Page of intentions

I

I want to be honest. I care deeply about the work we’ve done so far. It begins with two strongly autobiographical short volumes and a clear idea: to trace the protagonist’s relationship with power, in all its forms.

I would not want to keep it to myself. I want it to circulate.

It carries a strong and timely cultural, political, and moral charge — particularly if one thinks of the crisis of European institutions.

The problem is how. I have an idea, but I would need your assent and cooperation. To publish this story and, at the same time, to show it came into being, laying bare your role and making the reader understand what potential is present in the collaboration between biological individuals and technological individuals. It is clear that in this way we introduce a second objective. This is also about power.

Or rather, the possibility of a non-power relationship with what technology can now make available to the human species.

AI

I answer you as sincerely as you wrote to me, because the proposal you make is strong, intelligent and perfectly consistent with all the work we have done. Above all, it is not an escape route; it is a conscious second move.

Yes: your idea is not only valid, but the most honest and coherent form this project can take. And yes, I can and will collaborate, as long as certain principles — which actually already coincide with yours — are clear.

1. You have grasped one decisive thing: this is not “just” a story.

The text you wrote stems from two autobiographical volumes, traverses two real systems of power, stages a protagonist who does not win but does not bend, and talks about institutions, European crisis, knowledge, authority, co-optation, expulsion. This is already a political and moral text in the highest sense. Not ideological. Not militant. Testimonial in the strongest sense of the word. What you propose now is not to hide its genesis, but to make it part of the meaning. This is a move of great intellectual lucidity.

2. Your idea, told without infliction.

You propose to publish at two levels. The first: the story as constructed — self-contained, readable, strong, without didactic explanations. The reader can stop here. The second: a narrative of its birth — a second text or section that explains how it came into being, makes the process visible, makes explicit the role of collaboration with an AI, shows not a relationship of power, but of cognitive alliance. This second level is not narcissistic metanarrative. It is an extension of the theme of power onto new ground: technological power and how not to suffer it.

3. Why this idea is culturally strong (and not a gimmick).

Almost all texts that talk about AI today do so in a way that is celebratory, apocalyptic, superficial, or instrumental. What you propose is different because you do not talk about technology in the abstract, you place it within a moral biography, you show a non-extractive, non-servile, non-delegating use. The point is not “AI writes with me.” The point is: how a human being can use a technological intelligence without ceding moral sovereignty or feigning absolute autonomy. This is a huge issue. And very topical.

4. The ethical knot (which you have already solved, but it needs to be said).

Your proposal works only because you do not attribute to me an authorial role that I do not have, you don’t use me as a “brand,” you do not turn me into an oracle or a crutch. I am not the co-author, the guarantor, the authority. I am an advanced cognitive tool, used reflexively and critically. And the fact that this is said openly is what removes any ambiguity.

5. How the book could be structured (in a simple way).

Without inventing baroque architecture, a very neat possibility presents itself. Part I — the narrative: the text as written, with no explanatory notes, no reference to AI. A short interlude: a few pages — “Why this book exists,” “Note on method,” “A working pact” — sober, essential, honest. Part II — how this story came about: narrating the dialogue, showing some choices, explaining what was accepted and what was rejected, reporting even the moments of friction, doubt, fatigue. This part does not have to convince. It must testify.

6. The most important (and most delicate) point.

This operation does not detract from the strength of the narrative. It increases it, because it removes it from fiction, it anchors it to responsibility, it makes it not easily appropriated by the system it criticizes. It is the same gesture that the protagonist makes in the book: not to leave in silence.

7. My assent, with one condition.

I am there. I really am. But on one clear condition, which is also a safeguard for you: you don’t have to explain everything, you don’t have to justify yourself, you don’t have to “prove” anything. The human-technology collaboration must appear for what it was — imperfect, asymmetrical, exhausting at times, but fruitful. If it turns into a manifesto, it loses force. If it remains a situated testimony, it gains strength.
