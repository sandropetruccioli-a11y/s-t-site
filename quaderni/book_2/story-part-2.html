The Story – Part II

1.

The establishment of the European Center on Artificial Intelligence (ECAI) had taken years. Not for lack of ideas, but for the usual sum of hesitations, political fears, overlapping competencies. The urgency was clear to all, but no one seemed to want to be the first to take responsibility for it.

The turning point came when some figures in the scientific community decided to take public action. They did so not with proclamations, but with silent and continuous pressure, making it clear that the time for mediation was over.

Among them was Niels.

A world-renowned researcher, Niels had gone through the years without ever denying his academic origins. He shared much more than a university past with the protagonist: a common language, a shared impatience with shortcuts, a not naive belief in the power of ideas.

It was he who wanted the protagonist to attend the first international preparatory meetings. Not as a guest, but as an interlocutor. The meetings were held in a suspended atmosphere: big words, extreme caution, manic attention to vocabulary. They discussed architectures, models, governance. Few, however, really talked about power.

When, months later, the list of names destined to be part of the founding nucleus of the center was released, that of the protagonist surprised no one. It seemed, quite simply, the right place.

2.

The hall was bright, more than it needed to be. Clear walls, glass everywhere, a long oval table that seemed designed to have no head of the table, even though everyone knew exactly where to sit. The European Center for AI was not yet officially born, but it already flaunted an accomplished aesthetic, as if architecture could anticipate legitimacy.

At the first Board meeting, the powerful academic arrived last, with a leisurely pace. He did not have the air of someone presiding: rather, he looked like someone conceding his presence. Greetings were cordial, calibrated, with that measure that signals a hierarchy already internalized by all.

He took the floor unhurriedly.

He spoke of European vision, of historical responsibility, of global competition. China and the United States were evoked as almost natural presences, telluric forces that could only be measured against. He said the Center should be "neither academy nor enterprise," but something new, something higher. No one asked him what that really meant.

Then he began to assign roles.

He did so in an affable, almost fatherly tone. Each name was accompanied by a brief note of appreciation, a skill remembered, an achievement evoked. When he got to the delegate, his voice changed imperceptibly.

He praised him at length. He spoke of his "organizational ability," his "loyalty to the project," his "ability to hold different worlds together." It was disproportionate, insistent, almost compensatory praise. It became clear to the attentive observer that it was not true esteem: it was rather the payment of a debt, or the fulfillment of a promise made elsewhere, at another time, perhaps in another context.

The delegate nodded, composed. He did not thank immediately. He waited for the praise to wear off, then said a few words, all addressed to the "professor," never to the Board. It was already clear to whom he would respond.

When the powerful academic uttered the protagonist's name, there was a brief pause.

The words were of praise even then. He spoke of his scientific authority, his independence of judgment, the value his presence would bring to the project. But the tone was different: more formal, more distant. There was no warmth, no complicity. It sounded like a forced quote, as if imposed by reputation rather than conviction.

The protagonist listened without reacting. He had learned, long ago, to distinguish the necessary words from the true ones. He knew that recognition placed him not at the center, but at the visible margins: close enough to be useful, far enough away not to be dangerous. At least for now.

When the meeting broke up, the overall impression was positive. Everything appeared orderly, regulated, even promising. The project had finally taken shape, and with it a common language, at least on the surface.

The protagonist left the room in no hurry. He had no reason to be suspicious. That world was new, different from the one he had behind him. Here power presented itself openly, with statutes, organizational charts, procedures. There were no obvious shadows, no gross ambiguities.

The attitude of the powerful academic had seemed to him rhetorical, at times truncated, but he was not surprised: he had always considered it part of a role, a public posture. As for the delegate, he already knew his limitations and inclinations, but nothing that warranted a definitive judgment. He had learned, long ago, that people should be measured over time, not in first lines.

The task he had been given seemed clear and circumscribed. He had no further ambitions. He did not seek visibility or power. He thought the best way to be in that context was to stay exactly where he was, work well, cooperate, when necessary, intervene only when required.

If something didn't work, he would figure it out as he went along.

For now, there was no reason to force any reading.

3.

The delegate joined him in the hallway shortly after the meeting. He had a friendly, almost confidential tone, as if the formality of the Board was already behind him.

"I'm glad you're here," he said. "I think we can work well together."

The protagonist nodded. He answered in a few words, without emphasis. He felt no need to clarify anything: for him, collaboration was not a statement of intent, but a practice.

The delegate talked a lot. He explained, he anticipated, he reassured. He often used the professor's name, as if it were a guarantee always available. Every decision seemed to have already been made elsewhere, but presented as the result of a shared process.

At one point he mentioned the part of the project assigned to the lead.

"This is a sensitive area," he said. "We will have to move carefully. There are balances to be respected."

"Scientific balances?" asked the protagonist.

The delegate smiled, as one smiles at a naive question, without malice.

"Also," he replied. "But not only."

He added no more. He seemed to assume that the rest was implied, or perhaps not worth explicating.

The protagonist did not insist. This was not the time to ask for an explanation, and he felt no need to. He recorded that answer as one record incomplete data: something that would require verification.

When they said goodbye, he had a clear but not alarming feeling: the delegate was not bad, he was not even openly incorrect. He was simply used to moving in a world where decisions precede reasons, and justifications come later.

He thought that, if necessary, he might be able help him. Or at least, that's how it seemed to him at that moment.

4.

The first deadline had been set well in advance. It was not about anything definitive: a working draft, a proposed timetable, a preliminary document to be discussed with international partners.

When the day arrived, the document was not there.

The delegate spoke of unforeseen contingencies, unforeseen complexities, interlocutors who had asked for more time. All plausible, nothing verifiable. He promised that it would be ready by the following week.

The protagonist listened without intervening. He asked no questions. At that stage he still had no reason to doubt good faith. He mentally noted the postponement as a fact, not a signal.

A week later, the situation had not changed.

Board meetings began to take a repetitive form. Many words, few talking points. The delegate spoke at length, but without ever reaching an operational conclusion. Every issue was postponed to the next meeting, or subject to an outside opinion that never came.

When the powerful academic was present, the delegate seemed to stiffen. He would take refuge in vague formulas, avoiding exposure. Occasionally he would call out the protagonist's project, but always in general terms.

It was in one of those meetings that something new happened.

An outside researcher asked for precise clarifications on timing and responsibilities. The delegate hesitated, turned toward the powerful academic, then fell silent.

The silence grew longer.

The protagonist calmly intervened. Not to take charge, but to avoid embarrassment. He proposed a transitional, bounded, reversible solution. He did not promise results, but he indicated a path.

The meeting closed on that proposal.

From that day, without anyone openly stating it, meetings began, without anyone saying it, to wait for him to speak.

Meetings with international groups intensified. Universities, research centers, private laboratories. All demanded clarity, timing, stable interlocutors.

The delegate participated, but appeared more and more stymied. He avoided technical questions, deferred to central structures, promised answers that did not come.

The protagonist, on the other hand, spoke the language of business. He did not make promises he could not keep. He explained limits, constraints, real possibilities. He did not seek consensus; he produced it as a by-product.

It was not a charisma sought. It was a consequence.

At the end of one of those meetings, a German researcher said to him, almost distractedly:

"At least now we know who to talk to."

The delegate was standing nearby. He pretended not to hear.

In time, the protagonist noticed something that made him uncomfortable: people were beginning to address him before the delegate.

Not out of defiance, not out of strategy. Out of necessity.

The delegate noticed this in turn. But instead of welcoming the help, he stiffened. He defended his role in an increasingly formal, more bureaucratic way. He recalled procedures, competencies, hierarchies.

He was aware of the precariousness of his position. He knew that delegation was partial, temporary. Precisely because of this he experienced it as continuous scrutiny, and reacted by turning any collaboration into a threat.

The climate in the group changed. Not overtly, but deeply.

The work continued, but with an underground tension. Every decision became a minefield. Every proposal, a possible affront.

The protagonist realized then that the problem was no longer technical competence. It had become structural.

He had not sought that role. But the System, as it was organized, was giving it to him.

And someone, sooner or later, would react.

5.

In building the network of collaborations, the work was not just about ideas, models, algorithms, theoretical architectures. There were contracts, timelines, clauses. And above all, deadlines, which did not allow for postponements.

On that terrain the delegate moved contradictorily. Hesitant when decisions had to be made, arrogant when someone demanded accountability for delays. He repeated that the budget was under his control, that nothing could be activated without his signature. Formally that was true. Substantially, however, the signature that mattered was another: that of the powerful academic, who kept signing off with his eyes closed what the delegate brought him already "instructed."

In time, signs of impatience began to come from the administration. First in the form of technical reminders: requests for clarification, notes on the timing of contract activation, observations on the correctness of procedures. Then more explicitly: calls for compliance with the rules, the traceability of decisions, the need to avoid unjustified exceptions.

It was in that climate that the protagonist received an unexpected request.

A short, polite, almost disarming message. The director general of the Center was asking if it would be possible to meet. Nothing official, he had immediately clarified. Just a desire to meet him. "People I respect have spoken very highly of you. If you'd like, we could meet one evening, perhaps over dinner, to exchange some opinions."

The protagonist hesitated. Not out of mistrust, but out of caution. He accepted.

The dinner took place in a discreet restaurant away from the Center's headquarters. The initial tone was light. A few words about the project, the international context, the inevitable difficulties of a young institution. The director general listened a lot, spoke little.

Then, without emphasis, he changed register.

He began to list figures. Dates. Failed authorizations. Contracts never activated. Expenditures blocked without justification. All with a precision that left no room for improvisation. Each piece of data was accompanied by a document, an internal note, a cross-reference.

The protagonist listened in silence. He asked for clarification, he did not dispute. He was surprised, yes, but not incredulous. He understood that not everything could be attributed to incompetence or disorganization.

At one point the name of the powerful academic emerged. The director spoke about it in a different, more controlled tone. He said that something was broken. That a long-standing friendship was no longer enough to cover certain behaviors. That the Center could not afford such gray areas.

The allegations were serious. Perhaps not all of them verifiable. Perhaps not all of them true. But the whole drew a disturbing picture.

The protagonist did not take sides. He defended no one. He did not attack anyone. He merely said that that information struck him and that he needed time to reflect.

The director general nodded. Then, lowering his voice, he added a sentence that was not a formal proposal, but neither was it a simple statement:

"Together we could do very effective things."

He did not ask for an answer.

When the protagonist walked out of the restaurant, he had a new, crisp certainty. In the rooms and corridors of the headquarters, there was no discussion of plans or scientific visions.

A war was going on. And it involved no holds barred.

6.

Upon returning home, the protagonist did not turn on the light in the study. He left his jacket on the chair and sat down at the table without opening the computer. He did not need to review documents or mentally reconstruct the steps of the dinner. Everything was already clear.

The accusations themselves did not upset him so much. In every complex institution, he knew, partial versions circulate, data used as weapons, amplified truths and strategic omissions. He had long since learned not to take anything as definitive on first hearing.

What struck him was something else.

For the first time he was seeing the System from the inside, without the reassuring surfaces of formal transparency. Until then he had thought he was in a new environment, perhaps more regulated, less opaque than the one he had left behind. A System with clear rules, defined roles, declared conflicts.

Now he understood that it was not so simple.

There were no shadowy powers in the usual sense. No indistinct shadows. Everything was nominally visible, verbalized, logged. And for that very reason more insidious. The conflict was not through arbitrariness, but through the management of procedures. Not by direct abuse, but by control of timing. Not through violence, but through paralysis.

He thought back to the whispered phrase at the end of dinner: Together we could do great things.

It was not blackmailed. It was not even an explicit proposal. It was something more refined: a recognition of a strength and, at the same time, an invitation to use it in a specific direction.

He wondered, without complacency, why him of all people.

The answer did not reassure him. He had not been chosen for affinity. Nor for loyalty. But for impermeability. For the fact that he did not belong to any of the factions in the field. For this very reason he could become decisive.

He realized then that his position had changed, although formally nothing had changed. He had not sought power. But power had begun to take him into account.

And this troubled him more than any overt hostility.

He thought about the delegate. About his awkwardness, his baseless ambition. He thought of the powerful academic, of the confidence with which he continued to move as if the Center were a natural extension of his own domain. And finally, to the director general, the cool lucidity with which he had decided to break the balance.

Everyone, in their own way, had just given him a role that he had not asked for.

He also understood something else, with a clarity that left him no escape: any future choice, even inaction, would be read as a stance.

Staying in one's place, limiting oneself to work, would no longer suffice.

He turned off the hallway light, without having made any decisions. But with a new awareness: the new System was no less dangerous than the first. Only more elegant in hiding the moment when it asks you to choose.

7.

The delegate closed the door to his office with more force than necessary. He paused for a moment, his hand still on the handle, as if considering whether to open it again. Then he let it go.

He sat down without turning on the computer. For days he had avoided looking at the agenda: meetings piling up, deadlines postponed, promises made "pending confirmation." All formally correct. All, by now, fragile.

He thought back to the last Board meeting.

Nothing sensational had happened. No open contestation. No formal questioning of his role. Yet he had had the distinct feeling that something had changed. Not in the minutes. In the silences.

The protagonist had spoken little. As he always did. But when he had, the room had turned toward him without anyone realizing it. A precise sentence. A hypothetical solution. A reminder of the real times, not the announced ones.

Nothing that could be challenged.

And that was precisely the problem.

The delegate knew he was not up to the task, at least not the task as it was taking shape. But he also knew something else: that position had not been given to him out of technical competence. It was a political delegation. Temporary, yes. But renewable. If played well.

And to play it well required holding together two things that now eluded him: control of information and control of decisions.

With the powerful academic, the relationship had become ambiguous. It was no longer a relationship of trust, but one of necessity. When he called him, he always got an answer. But increasingly hasty. Less and less interested in details. He would sign, yes. But without really listening.

Worse still: for some time, he had been asking questions. Not about what was being done, but about who was speaking for him.

The delegate felt a feeling growing that he hated: being bypassed without being formally bypassed.

And the protagonist was there, at the center of this twist, without having done anything to conquer it.

This made him unbearable.

Not because he was arrogant. On the contrary. Because he was not seeking anything. He did not ask for favors. He did not build alliances. He made no promises. And yet everyone seemed to listen to him.

It was as if his mere presence produced an implicit confrontation. A confrontation that the delegate knew he was losing.

He finally opened the computer. He scrolled through unread emails. Requests for clarification from the administration. Solicitations about contracts. Questions about when external partners would be activated.

He knew he could answer them right away. That all it took was a signature, a green light, a minimal decision to get the mechanism moving again. But doing so meant exposure. It meant taking on a responsibility he did not want to share.

And above all, it meant acknowledging, implicitly, that someone else was already doing the work for him.

He shut everything down again.

At that moment a thought matured in him that he had avoided until then: if I cannot govern this project, I must at least keep it from slipping out of my hands.

It was not yet a plan. It was just a reaction. But it was the beginning of something.

He got up, picked up the phone and dialed a number he knew by heart.

"We need to meet," he said without preamble. "There's a problem. And it's not just about me."

As he hung up, he had a bitter certainty: from that moment on, no matter what happened, there would be no turning back.

8.

There was nothing solemn about the general manager's office. It was a functional, almost anonymous space with a large window overlooking the inner courtyard and a bookcase full of identical binders. No symbols of power. No rhetoric.

For this very reason, the protagonist immediately sensed that this convocation was not ordinary.

When he entered, he found the delegate and the powerful academic already seated.

The composition was unusual. Too unusual to be casual.

The delegate spoke softly, in a tone that was meant to be confident but betrayed nervousness. The powerful academic listened to him without interrupting, his hands intertwined on the table, his gaze fixed on an unspecified spot on the wall.

The director arrived last. He did not apologize for being late. He closed the door and got straight to the point.

"We have a problem," he said, sitting down. "And it's not a technical problem."

He picked up a folder, opened it, and slid some documents across the table.

Contracts. Preliminary agreements. Letters of intent.

Modest collaborations, at least on paper. But enough to move funds, to commit the center, to set precedents.

"These documents," the director continued, "were signed without adherence to established procedures. The timelines were not authorized. The coverages are not clear."

The delegate reacted immediately.

"These are strategic collaborations," he said. "If we lose time, we lose partners."

The director looked at him calmly.

"Strategic for whom?"

A short but charged silence followed.

The delegate raised his voice slightly. He spoke of urgency, international competitiveness, the need to be flexible. Every phrase was correct. Every topic he had heard before.

At one point, almost without realizing it, he turned to the powerful academic.

"I think on this point the professor can confirm," he said. "We have already discussed the importance of speeding up."

It was an instinctive gesture. A blanket request.

The powerful academic did not immediately respond.

The protagonist watched him carefully. He saw the color of his face change imperceptibly. He saw the hesitation, very rapid but real. He saw something he had not seen before: a limitation.

When he spoke, his voice was weaker than usual.

"I understand the reasons," he said. "But ... procedures exist for a reason."

It was not an endorsement. It was not a defeat. It was a step backward.

The delegate remained motionless, as if he did not immediately understand.

The director seized the moment.

"No one here wants to block the project," he said. "But from now on, any document that has economic or contractual implications will have to go through further scrutiny."

He turned back to the protagonist.

"And it will have to have his initials as well."

The protagonist felt the weight of that sentence before he even fully understood its consequences.

It was not a promotion. Not a formal delegation. It was something else: a responsibility without a title.

The delegate protested. He spoke of interference, confusion of roles, trust being questioned.

The director did not flinch.

"This is a temporary measure," he said. "To protect the institution."

The powerful academic said nothing. His silence at that moment was more eloquent than any stance.

When the meeting ended, no one had the impression that anything had been resolved.

But everyone understood that something was broken.

The protagonist walked out of the office with a new and disturbing certainty: without having looked for it, he had become an obligatory waypoint.

And he knew-with a lucidity that gave him no comfort-that from then on the conflict could no longer remain dormant.

9.

The delegate did not immediately return to his office.

He walked through the corridors of the headquarters without greeting anyone, as if he needed time before regaining an acceptable posture. He had the uncomfortable, almost physical sense that something had slipped out of his control in front of witnesses.

It wasn't the humiliation, not yet. It was worse: the exposure.

He had always known that his position was fragile. Delegated. Provisional. Conditional. But he had learned to live with this fragility by turning it into method: deferring, negotiating, pegging every decision to superior coverage. No clear error, no full responsibility.

Now that method had been cracked.

The director's phrase - "with the protagonist's initials" - continued to echo in his head. Not as a threat, but as a change in geometry.

He had not been overruled. He had been surrounded.

He finally sat down at his desk. He opened the computer, then closed it again without looking at the screen. He picked up the phone, put it down. Everything suddenly seemed slow, exposed, verifiable to him.

He thought of the powerful academic.

He had not defended him. Worse: he had hesitated.

That pallor - he had seen it too - was a sign that something had cracked higher up. Perhaps the director held more cards than he let on. Maybe times were changing.

And then there was the other.

The main character.

He didn't hate him. Not yet. But he perceived him as a growing anomaly.

He was not asking for anything. He was not negotiating. He did not seek alliances.

And because of that, he became unmanageable.

Every time he spoke in meetings, the discourse became clearer. Every time he took the floor, ambiguities were reduced. Every time he proposed a solution, someone had to decide.

This, in the System, was intolerable.

The delegate understood at that moment that collaboration was no longer an option. Not because the protagonist had done anything against him, but because it existed in the wrong way.

He took out a sheet of paper and began to write down some names.

Trusted academics. Old acquaintances. People who owed something. Some with respectable resumes, others less so, but all united by one thing: willingness.

He would not openly challenge the new arrangement. Not right away.

He would have created a parallel space. A place where decisions could be prepared before reaching the board. An informal circuit, presentable as "working group," "task force," "technical support."

Nothing illegal. Nothing overt.

By the time the powerful academic was ready to pull his weight again -- and he would be -- that circuit would appear as a ready-made solution.

What if the protagonist had raised objections?

Then, finally, he would have appeared for what it was easy to make him out to be: an obstacle, a slowdown, a man who did not understand the real dynamics of international power.

The delegate smiled for the first time since he had left the director's office.

It was not revenge. It was a survival strategy.

And in the System, as he had now learned, survival belonged to those who moved before the rules became final.

10.

He had waited for him at the end of the meeting, without insistence. When the others had left, the protagonist had slowly closed his notebook and approached the window. The powerful academic was putting the papers back in the folder with orderly, almost ritual gestures.

"Can I ask you something?" said the protagonist, without turning around.

"Sure." The tone was affable, as always. A tone that never really seemed to belong to the moment.

The protagonist turned around. There was no tension in his face, only watchful concentration.

"Some decisions made by the delegate," he said, "are not compatible with the Center's bylaws. I'm not talking about scientific evaluations. I'm talking about procedures, formal acts. He endorsed them."

The powerful academic barely smiled, as if recognizing a scrupulousness already seen elsewhere.

"You are very rigorous," he replied. "And that is a quality I appreciate."

"It's not a matter of rigor," the leading man replied. "It's a matter of shared rules. If we go around them at the beginning, they become optional. And at that point-"

"-at that point," the other politely interrupted him, "you have to be able to distinguish."

The protagonist remained silent, waiting.

"This is a complex project," the powerful academic continued. "International. Exposed. There are balances that do not always coincide with the letter of the rules."

"But they should coincide with their spirit," said the protagonist. There was no accusation in the voice. Just an observation.

The powerful academic sighed, as one who decides to shorten an overly long explanation. He sat down on the edge of the desk.

"You are right in principle."

The protagonist nodded imperceptibly.

"But in practice," added the other, "things are more complicated."

"In what sense?" asked the protagonist.

There was a brief pause. Not of hesitation, but of choice.

The powerful academic lowered his gaze for a moment, then raised it again.

"In life," he said slowly, "there are things you can't avoid doing."

The sentence hung between them, as if it needed air.

The protagonist did not immediately respond. He looked at the man in front of him: the confidence of gesture, the controlled calm, the total absence of embarrassment.

"I don't understand," he finally said.

The powerful academic made a small gesture with his hand, as if to indicate a broader horizon.

"Not everything is visible," he said. "Not everything is negotiable. Sometimes it's about accompanying processes, not governing them."

"What about the delegate?" asked the protagonist. "Is that also part of the things that can't not be done?"

For the first time, the smile disappeared.

"The delegate," replied the other, "performs a function."

"A function that exposes the Center to unnecessary risks."

The powerful academic stared at him. Not harshly, but with sudden curiosity, as if measuring a distance.

"He sees the risks," he said. "And that's why I value her. But he only sees those."

"What about her?" the protagonist asked.

There was a longer silence. Then:

"I see what would happen if some things were not done."

The protagonist understood at that moment that he was not talking about the delegate. Nor was he talking about the Center.

"I thank you for your frankness," he said.

"Frankness is always relative," replied the other, already taking the folder back.

As he walked out, the protagonist had the distinct feeling-not yet a judgment, not yet a condemnation-that from then on they would speak the same language only in appearance.

And that, sooner or later, someone would ask for the bill for those things you can't not do.

11.

The delegate entered without knocking. He closed the door behind him with a controlled, almost elegant gesture. The powerful academic was standing in front of the desk, intent on rereading a document.

"We need to talk," said the delegate.

The powerful academic did not immediately look up.

"I guess," he replied. "Sit down."

The delegate remained standing.

"No," he said. "I prefer to stay that way."

Finally, the other looked at him. Not with surprise. With a calmness that, to the delegate, was already an affront.

"At the meeting," he began, "you should have intervened."

The powerful academic tilted his head slightly.

"That's your opinion."

"It's not an opinion," replied the delegate, barely holding back his irritation. "You left me exposed. In front of the director. In front of him."

He did not pronounce the principal's name. There was no need.

"I did not see fit to intervene," said the other.

"Not appropriate?" The delegate took a half-step forward. "Do you have any idea what this means to me?"

"It means," replied the powerful academic in a flat voice, "that things are changing."

The delegate smiled, but it was a strained smile.

"Things are changing because you allow it."

"Or because I can no longer prevent it," retorted the other.

The delegate stared at him, incredulous.

"You put me there," he said. "You asked me to hold the project together. To do what needed to be done."

"I asked you to perform a function," the powerful academic replied. "Not to attract unwanted attention."

"Attention?" The delegate raised his voice. "These are impossible rules. Constraints that do not take reality into account. If I had followed everything to the letter, we wouldn't have even signed a contract."

"Maybe," said the other. "Or maybe we would have signed the right ones."

The delegate shook his head.

"Now repeat what he says," he said. "That's the point, isn't it?"

The powerful academic slowly approached the window.

"He," he said, "says nothing. Just observe."

"And you let him."

There was a short but charged silence.

"You had guaranteed me coverage," resumed the delegate. "You said that if there were any problems, you would intervene."

The powerful academic remained firm.

"There are things," he said, without turning around, "that can no longer be covered."

The delegate felt a cold chill. It was not anger. It was something more dangerous: the perception of displacement.

"Then tell me clearly," he said. "Am I still your man, or not?"

The powerful academic slowly turned around.

"You're still on the Board," he said. "And that should be enough for you."

The delegate laughed, dryly.

"It's not enough for me," he said. "Because he is taking away the ground under my feet. And you know it."

"Maybe," the other replied, "he's just showing where the ground was never there."

The delegate looked at him for a long time. Then he lowered his voice.

"Don't think you can play on two tables," he said. "If you drop me, I won't be the one to quietly step aside."

The powerful academic stared at him. For the first time, without indulgence.

"Be careful," he said. "Don't confuse loyalty with blackmail."

The delegate turned toward the door.

"Remember that," he said before leaving. "The things you can't not do-sometimes you pay for."

The door closed softly.

The powerful academic remained alone. And for the first time, silence seemed to him not a strategy but a crack.

12.

Summer had done its work. Meetings had thinned, messages had become shorter, tensions seemed to have receded like a tide that leaves only harmless debris uncovered. Nothing had really been resolved, but everything appeared, at least on the surface, brought back within an acceptable form.

The protagonist had felt that reprieve as a fact, not a promise. He knew that the System, when silent, does not quiet down: it takes its time.

It was in those days that the news came.

One of the research institutions that had initially steadfastly refused to join the European project seemed to have changed its position. Cambridge. Not just any peripheral membership, but a weighty, well-known, respected facility that until a few months earlier had looked at the project with a mixture of distance and skepticism.

The return was not announced with fanfare. It happened in stages: a cordial letter, an initial technical exchange, draft memoranda of understanding that circulated among offices and secretariats with unusual speed. Some, with a certain smugness, attributed the result to the powerful academic's diplomatic skills, his international relations, the symbolic capital he still knew how to spend effectively.

The delegate, for the first time in weeks, seemed to be reborn.

When the decision to co-opt a tutor at one of Cambridge's colleges onto the project board was announced, the news was presented as a collective success.

Formally, his academic role was modest. He did not lead departments; he did not lead large groups. Yet in the right corridors, his name circulated with a frequency that could not be explained by scientific merit alone.

The protagonist watched him carefully from the first informal meeting and was genuinely intrigued. The man was everything the delegate was not. Quick wit, subtle irony, an almost instinctive ability to read people even before structures. He had a hybrid background-mathematics, history of science-and a way of speaking that sought not consensus but understanding.

It was during an informal break away from the official table that something unexpected happened.

The tutor seemed to have understood before anyone else.

He did not ask for anything. He made no allusions. He merely observed the workings of the Board, the silences of the powerful academic, the stubborn hesitations of the delegate. Then, casually, he began to talk to the protagonist. Not about strategy, not about alliances. Of real problems, of timing, of method. About science.

The understanding was immediate, but not emphatic. One of those friendships that one does not declare: one recognizes.

The protagonist, who up to that point had maintained a cautious vigilance, felt something change. Not in the System, but in his relationship with it. For the first time since entering the Center, he had the feeling that that architecture could work. Not because it was perfect, but because there was now someone with whom he could really share the responsibility of thinking.

In Cambridge, in the days that followed, they worked intensively. Papers were discussed on their merits. Objections addressed without theatrics. The economic part, while significant, was treated as what it should be: a condition, not an end.

The protagonist came out convinced. Not reassured - convinced.

Convinced that the deal was good. Convinced that the project had finally found a credible balance. Convinced, thanks in part to the presence of the tutor, that the ambiguities of the System could be governed from within.

The signing of the preliminary protocol took place in one of the historic rooms of the prestigious College. Afterwards, the convivial lunch. Prominent academics, measured speeches, a concluding toast with a vintage champagne that sealed the understanding as one seals things destined to last.

The protagonist long remembered that moment. The light filtered through the ancient windows. The feeling, rare, of not being out of place.

Twenty years of experience had taught him not to trust enthusiasm. But this time it was not enthusiasm: it was rational, shared conviction.

Twenty days later, the powerful academic received a letter.

It was brief. Cold. Definitive.

In an impersonal formula, the research institute announced that, following a "thorough evaluation," the conditions for participation in the European Center project no longer existed. The reasons given were economic. General. Inconsistent.

The letter was immediately forwarded to the entire Board.

The protagonist read it twice. The second more slowly.

Nothing in those lines explained Cambridge. Nothing explained the protocol. Nothing explained the toast.

It was at that moment that he understood - not what was happening, but that something much bigger had already happened, elsewhere, without witnesses.

And that what was about to deflagrate was not a mistake, nor a failure.

It was a choice.

Only later did the protagonist realize what had actually been interrupted.

It was not an immediate rapprochement. At first they exchanged a few sentences, almost marginal, during breaks in meetings. Side remarks, ironic, sometimes even incongruous with the agenda. The protagonist quickly grasped that these were not improvisations: they were signals of recognition.

The tutor spoke quickly, seemingly haphazardly, but with a lucidity that left no room for misunderstanding. He did not seek consensus, he did not propose himself as an ally. He simply said what he thought, without calculating the effect. That was rare in that context.

It was during an evening walk, after a day of meetings, that the distance cancelled. They walked aimlessly, talking about something else entirely: ancient mathematics, formal languages, how certain logical structures survived the centuries by only changing their names. The protagonist realized he was saying things he normally kept silent. Not out of prudence, but out of economy: it was not worth explaining what would not be understood.

With him, however, there was no need to simplify.

The tutor listened with real, not instrumental, attention. He asked dry, sometimes unsettling, but never pointless questions. At one point he said, almost absentmindedly, "In here it's not the project that counts. It's who decides when something can be said and when not."

The protagonist did not immediately respond. Not because he disagreed, but because he recognized in that sentence a thought, he had formulated many times, without ever uttering it. From that moment, a form of understanding was established between them that did not need to be stated.

Over the next few days, the relationship solidified. They talked openly about the tensions in the Board, the ambiguities of the delegate, the opaque role of the powerful academic. Not to complain, but to understand. The tutor made no secret of his sympathies: he was on the side of the project, not careers. And this, in the System, already amounted to a stance.

The protagonist, who rarely granted trust, realized this only later: he was entrusting that man with thoughts he had never shared with anyone in that context. He was not seeking protection or alliances. He was looking for truth. And for a short time, he had the illusion that it was possible.

Therefore, when the news of his resignation came, the blow was not only institutional. It was personal.

No plausible explanation. No prior signals. Just a correct, impersonal communication that closed any possibility of dialogue. The protagonist reread those lines several times, looking for a detail that was not there. A dissonance, a crack. Nothing.

He realized then that the escape had been a choice. Not a mistake, not a sudden weakness. A lucid choice, perhaps necessary, but irrevocable.

And that hurt him more than any explicit attack.

For he had not lost an ally. He had lost the only voice that, in that System, had spoken to him without masks.

What had happened in that short period of time was never clarified. No one could reconstruct a sequence; no one could identify responsibility.

Only one thing appeared certain. Knowing the seriousness and moral rigor of the interlocutors, someone had to have introduced more than a grain of sand into the gear. Not to slow it down, but to block it.

This was not an economic issue. The reasons given in the letter were too general, too patently unfounded to be true. There must have been different demands, made indirectly, conditions that the institute could never accept without distorting itself.

Some spoke openly of a vendetta. Others thought of the delegate and the informal group he had built around him, a network that was never official but always operational. No hypothesis could be proven. All appeared plausible.

Adding to the disorientation came, a few days later, the announcement of the tutor's resignation. No convincing explanation, no words to clarify. Only a correct, impersonal, definitive formula.

It was then that the protagonist understood. Not what had happened, but that something had happened. And that, from that moment, it was no longer a matter of defending a project, but of figuring out how to act in a System capable of striking without leaving a trace.

13.

The protagonist had never been prone to opaque explanations. Not out of naiveté, but out of method. He had always thought that recourse to opaque explanations was often an alibi: an elegant way of foregoing a rigorous understanding of the facts. He preferred to stick to the data, even when they were inconvenient, and to formulate hypotheses only insofar as they served to connect what appeared dissonant.

The Cambridge affair, however, forced him to stop.

Not because he suddenly believed in conspiracies or unseen plots, but because what had happened did not allow itself to be explained with the tools that had previously sufficed. The sequences did not add up. The official reasons were flimsy. The timelines, incompatible with any serious decision-making process. And above all: the people involved were not amateurs or adventurers. They were individuals of proven intellectual and moral soundness.

For weeks he tried to accurately reconstruct the course of events. He reread documents, compared dates, went over conversations, tried to talk to those who might have known something. But no one really knew. And those who might have known were silent. Not with hostility, but with that form of caution that comes not from immediate fear, but from a long habit of silence.

On one occasion he managed to elicit a sentence. Not an explanation, just an indication: "If you want to understand, you must look in your own house."

He asked no more. He knew when an answer was already all that would be granted.

Added to this was another wound, more subtle and more painful. The sudden withdrawal of the tutor. Not the act itself - which he could have understood, even justified - but the manner. The coldness, the absence of a private word, the failure of the frankness he had believed to be mutual.

In time he was forced to admit what he had initially rejected: the man had a split posture. Not a double morality, perhaps, but two registers of behavior that did not communicate with each other. And he had only had access to one.

This did not make him distrustful. It made him more rigorous.

He understood that there were contexts in which correct procedures were not enough, and others in which formal transparency served to conceal decisions already made elsewhere. Above all, he understood that the problem was not discovering what could not be seen, but learning to recognize the points at which reality ceased to be questionable.

From then on, his attitude changed imperceptibly but permanently. He did not close doors. He did not reduce contacts. He did not surround himself with loyalists. He did the opposite: he increased the level of precision. He demanded that every step be traceable. That every decision had explicit accountability. That every agreement be publicly defensible, without exception.

Not because he thought he could defend himself against everything. But because he understood that rigor, in certain systems, is already a form of exposure.

This realization prepared him for subsequent choices. It did not show him a sure way - they do not exist - but it made clear to him the price of each option. When, later, he would be entrusted with greater responsibilities, he could not say he did not know. He had seen enough. He had understood enough.

And he had decided, once and for all, on what plane he would place his answers.

14.

It was not a sudden decision.

The powerful academic had long since become a problem. Not because of his scientific achievements-which no one questioned-but because of the way he had progressively bent the Center to his own personal, academic and relational trajectories. A casual, sometimes unscrupulous way that had ended up coagulating a silent but substantial opposition.

No one, however, had any interest in opening a trial. No one wanted to expose the System to head-on confrontation, revelations, collateral damage. The problem had to be solved quietly, scaling the man down and safeguarding the institution.

When one of the most influential advisers took the floor at the meeting, the mood changed imperceptibly. He was a figure who had always been out of the picture, unconnected to the power networks, unassailable precisely because he could not be traced to any faction.

The tone was firm, measured, devoid of any polemical accent.

"Professor," he said, "the issue we are called upon to address today is neither about the results of the project nor its scientific relevance. It is about the governance structure of the Center."

He paused briefly, enough to make it clear that this was not a personal opinion.

"You cannot be, at the same time, the controlled and the controller."

The sentence fell like a notarial act. It did not accuse, it did not interpret, it left no room for rebuttal on the merits. It captured a condition that the System had decided not to tolerate any longer.

From then on, everything was a consequence.

At that point an immediate problem arose: the continuity of the project.

For a few seconds no one spoke. It was clear to everyone that it was not a matter of choosing the best, nor the most authoritative, nor the most ambitious. What was needed was someone who:

was not compromised with the dynamics that had just exploded,

was internationally legitimate,

and above all, did not pose a threat to the overall balance.

It was in that vacuum that the name of the protagonist emerged. Not as a proposal, but as an observation.

There was no enthusiasm. There were no congratulations.

Only a cold, almost relieved consensus.

15.

The decision was not communicated to him immediately. Not out of forgetfulness, nor out of administrative negligence: the delay had its own function, as if time might still be working in someone's favor.

In the days following the board meeting, nothing seemed to change. The powerful academic continued to act as if that discussion had never taken place. In informal conversations, in e-mail exchanges, even in brief meetings in the corridors of the headquarters, he made no mention of what had been decided. He spoke of the project to the future, as if the leadership was still firmly in his hands, as if reality had to conform to his willingness not to recognize it.

The protagonist learned of the appointment indirectly, almost accidentally. An administration official, with the air of someone who takes for granted something that is already known to everyone by now, asked him for clarification on a contractual passage. Only then did he realize that his name appeared as the project manager. A few days later, the new contract was delivered to him, promptly signed by the administrative director, as if any further delay could become a risk.

Within the Center, the reaction was ambiguous. A substantial part of the academic component greeted the news with ill-concealed annoyance. Too young, too alien to established genealogies, too disinclined to the rituals of representation: the protagonist was perceived as a foreign body, tolerated more out of necessity than conviction. There was no frontal opposition, but a widespread expectation of error, of confirmation that that choice had been risky.

Elsewhere, however, the opposite happened. In the international network of collaborators - scholars, researchers, outside centers that had followed with growing frustration the uncertainties of the previous phase - the news was greeted as a relief. At last there was a clear, reliable interlocutor, capable of making decisions and keeping commitments. Responses came quickly, deadlines were met, and content was back at the center of the work.

Meanwhile, rumors began to circulate in the corridors of headquarters. There was talk of an imminent closure of the project, of a drastic downsizing of the Center, of a future already written elsewhere. These were the last weapons of those who had not accepted the decision, attempts to wear down its effects before they could produce results.

The System, as a whole, watched. It did not intervene, correct, or deny. It merely recorded the progress of the work, as if waiting for the moment when time itself would deliver its verdict.

The project went ahead. And in the years that followed, without fuss or proclamation, it met its timetable and goals, producing results that no delaying maneuver could obscure any longer.

16.

There was no specific moment when anyone uttered the word crisis. If they had, it would have seemed premature, perhaps even ungenerous. Yet, for weeks, something had stopped working as before.

Documents kept arriving with regularity: interim reports, progress notes, extension requests. Everything was formally correct, drafted in the neutral, reassuring language of the institution. But read side by side, those texts returned a different picture: slipping deadlines, reformulated goals, interdependencies that made each delay contagious.

The protagonist noticed this almost by accident, putting his own project materials in order. His work was done: validated, discussed, documented. The results were there, and they were solid. They could have formed a basis on which to build more, accelerate, correct routes. Instead, nothing was happening. No decisions followed success. No operational consequences.

It was as if the System did not know what to do with a project that had worked.

Meetings multiplied. Preparatory meetings, technical tables, "strategic alignment" sessions. The lexicon was always the same: integration, benchmarking, changed context. No one talked about failures. No one talked about choices.

The protagonist listened. He did not intervene out of controversy, but for clarity. He brought data, proposed practical solutions, pointed out administrative knots that could be untied without trauma. His remarks were carefully received, noted, sometimes even praised. But then they would get lost. Absorbed, diluted, postponed to a later stage that never came.

He began to sense that the problem was not technical. It was temporal.

Some projects were asking for time. Others were asking for protection. Still others hoped that time would solve what one did not have the courage to face. The Center seemed to move like an organism that avoids pain by displacing it, never curing it.

It was in a seemingly ordinary meeting that he felt the change. There were no heated tones or sharp stances. But, for the first time, someone said -- in a flat, almost distracted voice -- that perhaps it would be necessary to "rethink the overall direction."

The phrase remained suspended. No one challenged it. No one immediately developed it. And for that very reason it weighed.

The protagonist realized then that it was no longer a matter of making what existed work better. A different transition was being prepared: the need to decide what the Center should become, and what it should give up being.

In the weeks that followed, positions began to emerge more sharply, while remaining covered by a shared language. There were those who spoke of "temporary" downsizing, those who evoked stronger alliances, and those who stressed external constraints as if they were a definitive justification. The protagonist continued to argue that the problem was not scientific quality but structure: a machine that was too heavy, traversed by baronies and excessive prudence, incapable of truly valuing young researchers.

His words were not being attacked. They were being isolated.

When the call came for a larger meeting, with an unusually short agenda, he understood that the dialogic phase was coming to an end. The discussion would no longer be about how to do things, but about who had the title to point them in the direction.

He felt no anger, no enthusiasm. Only a lucid awareness: whatever decision was made, it would not be neutral. And, above all, it would not be painless.

The crisis, at last, had found a form.

17.

The council room was larger than necessary. An oval table, light wood, clean surfaces, screens turned off. No one had brought paper documents. There was no need for them.

The president opened the meeting without preamble. He said only that the agenda item was the strategic sustainability of the Center in the medium term.

Everyone knew what that meant.

An initial, brief, technical speech followed. A counselor who hardly ever spoke. He exposed numbers already known: delays, slippages, asymmetries between projects. He did not accuse anyone. He did not propose solutions.

He merely said:

The current model is no longer consistent with the international context.

No one replied.

It was then that a woman sitting not far from the protagonist took the floor. He represented one of the large institutional funders. Her tone was calm, almost gentle.

We are not here to discuss the scientific value of what has been done. The results are there. But the problem is not the quality. It is speed. It is the ability to make decisions. It is predictability.

He paused for a moment.

A Center that cannot guarantee definite time becomes irrelevant, even if it is brilliant.

That was position A, said without proclamation.

Another speech followed, more articulate. An elegant man, used to speaking in international contexts.

We need to ask ourselves whether it still makes sense to think of this Center as an autonomous hub. The world is not waiting. The big platforms are already in place. Alliances exist. It is not a matter of giving something up, but of fitting into a larger value chain.

He never pronounced names. There was no need.

Structured collaborations, sharing of infrastructure, convergence of standards.

That is the language of our time.

That was position B. Presented as inevitable modernity.

Then the president looked in his direction. Not with emphasis. With a kind of formal duty.

Professor, would you like to speak?

The protagonist stood up slowly. He had no notes. He was not seeking consensus.

It seems to me that we are discussing as if failure is a given.

Some people stiffened.

It is not. Projects work. Collaborations exist. Results are recognized. The problem is not the science. It is the way we have decided to administer it.

He paused briefly.

If the Center is slow, it is not because Europe is incapable. It is because we have overlapped levels of decision-making, protected rents, turned caution into paralysis.

No theatrical gestures. Just precise words.

The real choice is not between closing or aligning. It is between continuing to run research as an administrative apparatus or returning it to those who produce it.

Some took notes. Others did not.

Reduce bureaucracy. Make room for young people. Accept the risk. This Center can be a third pole only if it stops behaving like a sum of small fiefdoms.

He did not conclude with an appeal. He sat down.

The president thanked everyone. He said that the positions were clear. That the Council would deliberate.

The silence that followed was not hostile. It was definitive.

No one asked for a recess. No one proposed mediation.

The meeting closed like that. With no visible votes. Without declared winners.

18.

Outside that room, the verdict had already been read.

To the east, in a Beijing office, an official barely smiled:

They did not have enough time.

To the west, an executive of a large platform commented:

It was a good experiment. But too European.

An American researcher, more honest, said:

They had the courage to try it. But they didn't want to get their hands all the way dirty.

No one talked about plots. No one talked about treachery.

They talked about opportunities. Of spaces opening up. Of talents coming back into circulation.

Inside the Center, meanwhile, the corridors were silent. Someone had already understood. Someone was pretending not to.

The protagonist knew this without the need for official communications: his position had not been defeated because he was weak, but because he was demanding a change that the System could not afford.

And that, in some places, is already a fault.

The news did not come as official communication. It came as important things always do: by discard, by omission, by tone.

In the halls of the Center there was no agitation. There was something worse: a restrained calm.

A young researcher came out of a meeting room with his laptop under his arm. He looked at the protagonist, hinted at a smile that quickly faded.

Is it true?

He did not specify what.

The protagonist did not answer immediately. He made a vague gesture, as if to say it depends on what you mean.

The boy lowered his voice:

They told us to suspend everything. Just for now. But.

He did not finish the sentence.

In a nearby office, two doctoral students were talking in low voices. One said:

I had just sent the latest version of the code.

The other replied:

You keep it. Don't upload it yet.

No anger. Just sudden caution.

An administrative man, who usually avoided any involvement, stopped the protagonist near the coffee machine.

I'm sorry.

He said it as one says when one is forced to choose a neutral formula.

It's not his fault.

And he added, after a moment:

But here now...it's better to be still.

In a larger room, someone had already erased a blackboard. Traces of formulas still visible under the sponge. As if it were unclear whether they should be preserved or removed altogether.

A senior researcher, one of those who had always kept their distance, said aloud:

Maybe it's for the best. It had become too ... ideological.

No one contradicted him. No one approved of him.

The protagonist walked through the central corridor. He noticed that some doors were closed. Not out of hostility. Out of self-protection.

A young man who had followed his project from the beginning approached him. He said nothing. He just handed him a flash drive.

In the case.

Then he walked away.

Later, walking past the room where operational meetings had been held for years, the protagonist saw that the project name tag had not been removed. It was still there. But slightly shifted.

He realized then that the decision had not destroyed anything. It had done something more subtle: it had interrupted a trajectory.

There had been no public defeat. There were no culprits.

Only people who, suddenly, no longer knew whether it was permissible to continue believing.

And that, in a place of research, is the clearest sign that something is over.

19.

After that meeting, the climate changed without anything being said openly.

The emails became more formal. The summonses more sparse. The responses slower.

In subsequent meetings, positions were no longer discussed: they were merely recorded. The key words changed: from project to sustainability, from research to governance, from vision to compatibility.

The protagonist understood that decisions were no longer being made about what to do, but about who could still speak.

This was not a personal attack. It was something colder and more definitive: a gradual subtraction of space.

It was then that he tried his riskiest card.

On the strength of his achievements, he prepared a new proposal. Not a contestation, but a relaunch: a project that took Option C and translated it into operational form. Less bureaucracy, less waste, more scientific autonomy, more young people.

He sent it to the Board discreetly, without solicitation.

He was not summoned to explain it. He received no questions. There was no discussion.

One afternoon, in the corridors of the headquarters, an official stopped him with embarrassment. He told him that the decision was negative. There were no reasons to communicate. That was all.

The protagonist understood. But he did not stop.

20. There was no communication.

The protagonist noticed this one morning as he entered the Center with his usual badge. The reader made a short, unfamiliar sound. A second later, the door opened anyway.

He made a mental note of it without giving it any weight.

In his office, however, something had changed. The desk was the same, the books in place, the computer turned on. But a folder was missing on the table: the one with the project logo, always there for years.

He thought of a distraction. He opened his e-mail.

There were messages. Lots of them. But no summons.

He scrolled through the shared agenda: a Board meeting had been moved. It was no longer listed among the participants.

No explicit cancellation. Simply, his name was not there.

He walked out into the hallway. He passed a colleague with whom he had worked for a long time. She stopped, greeted him warmly, then lowered her gaze.

I don't know if...

He interrupted himself.

Maybe it's just temporary.

The protagonist nodded. He asked no more.

Later, a message from the administration:

Due to space reorganization needs, your office will be temporarily reallocated.

Temporarily. An elastic word that meant nothing and contained everything.

The new office was smaller. Interior. Without windows.

On the door, his name had not yet been posted.

Over the next few days, the signs piled up with almost elegant precision.

Requests for advice no longer came. Documents were sent to him for information, when they were now final. A remote access to a shared folder was suddenly unauthorized.

He asked for an explanation. Always in a calm tone. Always without accusation.

He received correct answers. Never complete ones.

This is a transitional phase.

We are redefining competencies.

Nothing personal.

One afternoon, walking past the meeting room, he heard his own voice coming from inside. Someone was quoting an idea of his. Without attributing it.

He felt no anger. Only a strange feeling of strangeness, as if he were listening to a language he had once spoken fluently.

The last signal came quietly.

A short message:

For optimization of decision flows, the project will be followed directly by the Board.

No reference to him. No formula of thanks.

He realized then that the exclusion was not a punishment. It was a correction of trajectory.

The System was not kicking him out. It was making him irrelevant.

And that was, for him, more definitive than any formal act.

He closed the computer. He picked up some books.

He left the others behind.

Before leaving, he looked around the office once more. Not with nostalgia. With lucidity.

He had gone through another System. And, once again, he was coming out noiselessly.

Then he had a hesitation. He paused to reflect. He decided to play one last card. The one he did not like to use. He asked for help.

21.

The call is set with difficulty. Niels is on the other side of the world, between an airport and a lecture hall, his time measured and his voice already tired before he even speaks. When the screen lights up, the protagonist has the impression of seeing a familiar and at once distant face again: the same quick smile, the same way he tilts his head, but his gaze is elsewhere, as if already projected to the next sentence, the next engagement.

At first they talk about marginal things. A convention, a city rushed through, a joke about the time zone. It is a protective language, almost a ritual of mutual protection. Both know why they are there, but neither wants to be the first to force the threshold.

Then the protagonist gets to the point. He does not raise his voice, he does not argue as in a council or meeting. He tells. He tells what has happened, what is happening now, what is about to happen. He talks about the Center, about the crisis that is not scientific but political, about the three positions that are now crystallized, about the impossibility of a third way that would also be the only sensible one. He talks about his project, about the proposal rejected without discussion, about the air that has suddenly become rarefied around him.

Niels listens. He nods. Every now and then he murmurs an "I understand," a "yes, I thought so," a "you are not the only one." These are words that do not open, do not close, do not promise anything. The protagonist notices, but he moves on.

He gets to the point that weighs on him the most. He says he is not asking for protection or sponsorship. He says he doesn't want shortcuts. But that, for once, the Center would need a voice that doesn't come from within, someone to publicly remind it why it came into being, what it was supposed to be. He says that that someone, if he exists, is Niels.

On the other side of the screen falls a silence different from previous ones. It is not distracted; it is not technical. It is a full silence. Niels shifts in his chair, looks off-screen, perhaps at a window, perhaps at someone waving for him to hurry up.

When he speaks, he does so in a few sentences. Measured. He says that the situation is complex. That the times are not favorable. That certain balances, once broken, do not reassemble without collateral damage. He says he admires the consistency of the protagonist, that the work done was extraordinary, that no one can really erase it.

But he doesn't say yes. And he doesn't say no.

It is this, more than anything else, that affects the protagonist. The suspension. The retreat into the neutral language of one who has learned that silence is a form of survival.

The call closes with a cordial, almost affectionate greeting. Niels promises to "keep informed," to "see what can be done," to "stay in touch." Weightless promises, uttered without bad faith.

When the screen goes off, the protagonist sits for a few more seconds. He is not angry. He is not disappointed in the common sense of the word. It is as if something, at last, has come into focus.

He understands that even Niels, with all his authority, has chosen the point at which to stop. He understands that not all forms of withdrawal take the form of attack. Some are made of caution, of distance, of well-behaved silences.

And he understands, above all, that from that moment on he can no longer expect anything that does not depend solely on him.

The call did not change the course of events. But it ended the last illusion.

The exit was not announced. There was no official communication.

Simply, his name stopped appearing. Decisions were made elsewhere. Projects went on without him.

Within the Center, the academic component reacted with annoyance, then with cautious acceptance. He was young, too autonomous, too external. Outside, in the international scientific community, the reaction was the opposite: disbelief, respect, silent solidarity.

22. Much later, the protagonist was living in a country house. Few affections. Good readings. The patient wagging of his vizsla.

He had metabolized almost everything. He knew that some wounds do not heal.

He felt the need, however, to close the circle.

He wrote a letter to Niels. He knew he would get no reply.

He wrote about the college years, the shared ideals, the plans. He wrote about power and its masks. He wrote about his own affair without accusation or recrimination.

He thanked Niels for the friendship. For the opportunity he received. For allowing him to perform a prestigious task.

He ended the letter without asking for anything.

It was not a request. It was a bequest.

Dear Niels,

I put off this letter for a long time, not out of hesitation, but because I needed time to do its work. Some things can only be understood when they no longer demand an immediate response or a stance.

I am not writing to you to explain what has happened. You know it, at least in its essentials. And you also know that many of the real causes are never visible all the way through, because power does not like to be told: it prefers to act by minimal additions, by imperceptible scraps, by well-distributed silences.

Rather, I am writing to say that I have understood.

I understood that the System - whatever form it takes - is never an abstract entity. It is made of people, of fears, of conveniences, of unstable balances that defend themselves by presenting themselves as necessities. I saw it in my first university experience, when power masqueraded behind tradition, belonging, dark alliances that demanded obedience in exchange for protection. And I saw it again, in a different but no less effective form, when in the Center power presented itself as governance, strategy, geopolitical realism, historical urgency.

In both cases the mechanism was the same: tolerate difference as long as it remains harmless, expel it when it becomes criterion.

I never thought of myself as an opponent. I have not been one. I never sought conflict, nor did I attribute to myself a role that I was not entitled to. I simply did what I thought was right to do, in the place and time I was in. I tried to tell the truth when it was inconvenient, to abide by the rules when others bent them, to keep commitments even when doing so was not convenient.

I knew this would come at a cost. I was never naïve. But there is a difference between knowing that something will be difficult and accepting that it should not be attempted. For me, not attempting it would have been worse.

I do not regret anything. Neither the missed opportunities, nor the careers I could have pursued, nor the protections I was offered. I have had the rare privilege of working with extraordinary people, of watching young free intelligences grow, of building something that-even if it will be downsized or removed-has left real traces. That is enough.

I know that you too have had to choose your silences. I do not reproach you for anything. Every biography has its balance point, and yours is different from mine. I am grateful to you, sincerely, for the trust you have placed in me, for the friendship you have granted me, for calling me to a task that has been among the most challenging and honest of my life.

If I am here today, far from the places of power, it is not out of retreat or defeat. It is because I have finished what I had to do. I went through the Systems without becoming a part of them, and this - I know now - was the only way I could inhabit them without betraying myself.

Perhaps I was wrong to believe that institutions could stand the test of consistency to the end. Maybe I was right to try anyway. In any case, I would do it all over again.

I wish you all the best. And whatever happens, know that I do not consider any of what has been as time wasted.

With friendship,

[signature].

He had not told him everything.

He had not told him how exhausting it had been to remain impermeable. How, at times, loneliness had been a choice and not a consequence. How much he had longed, at least once, for someone to say to him: stop, that's enough, you've done enough.

He had not told him that what had weighed most on him was not the exclusion, nor the loss of role, nor the hostility masquerading as institutional fairness. It had been seeing how intelligent, prepared, even honest people had accepted without rebellion a logic they knew was wrong. Not out of malice, but out of weariness. Out of fear of being alone.

After all, he thought, power almost always wins that way.

He had never believed that he could change the System. He had believed, this yes, that it was possible to go through it without allowing himself to be deformed. Now he knew that even that had a price. But it was a price he recognized as his own.

He folded the letter carefully. He did not wait for a reply.

What needed to be said had been said. To himself, before anyone else.

The letter remained on the desk.

Outside, the light was changing. It was no longer broad daylight, but not yet evening. The wind was barely moving the trees around the house.

The dogs crossed the lawn unhurriedly, as if they had always known the rhythm of that place. One stopped, looked toward him, then resumed walking.

There was nothing to decide.

Only to stay.
