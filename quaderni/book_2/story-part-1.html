The Story – Part I

1.

The protagonist arrived at that university without really having decided to do so.

It wasn't a destination, but a stop along the way. One of those solutions that you accept because they solve an immediate problem, not because they fit into a plan. He had other plans, other possible directions. That place was not on his mental map except as a parenthesis.

The offer had come to him in an ordinary, even impersonal way. A competition, a phone call, a series of formal steps that had not required any particular mediation on his part. Thinking about it later, this should have made him suspicious. But at the time, it seemed like a sign of normality.

He arrived with few expectations. He knew he wouldn't stay long. He even told himself this with a certain serenity: I'll do my job, then we'll see. He had no intention of intervening in the context. Nor of changing it. Nor of interpreting it.

He spent the first few months observing. Not with suspicion, but with attention. He had learned early on that every institution has its own language, made up more of silences than words. He tried to understand it, to decipher its basic codes. But what he saw did not match his expectations.

Things that were normal for his colleagues — informal exchanges, decisions made elsewhere, unwritten agreements — appeared to him as obvious violations. Not scandalous, not sensational. Simply unseemly. He took note of them without comment.

He wasn't there to judge and above all, he wasn't there to fight.

The protagonist did his job conscientiously. He prepared his lessons, received students, and attended the necessary meetings. He spoke little. When he did speak, it was to clarify a point, never to influence a decision. He didn't seek consensus or alliances.

For many, this was already an anomaly.

Some colleagues considered him distant. Others thought he was naive. Some soon began to call him "rigid," without him noticing. Not for what he did, but for what he didn't do: he didn't ask for favors, he didn't exchange protections, he didn't adhere to implicit rituals.

Sometimes, without meaning to, he would speak his mind. Not in a provocative way. He simply called things by their name. When this happened, the reaction was always the same: a brief silence, then a change of subject. No one openly contradicted him. But no one picked up on what he had said either.

He realized this. And he drew a cautious conclusion: this is not my world.

He didn't complain about it. On the contrary, this estrangement protected him. It allowed him to remain on the sidelines, not to get involved in dynamics he didn't understand and, ultimately, didn't care about. He was convinced that time would run its course. That he would leave before that distance became a problem.

2.

The conflict broke out elsewhere. Not for idealistic reasons. Not because of different visions of the university. But because of base interests, opaque intrigues, and balances that had been upset.

At first, the protagonist only perceived echoes of it: voices in the corridors, sudden meetings, looks that changed when he entered a room. Then things became more explicit. Complaints. Lawyers. Stamped papers.

The judiciary entered the scene. Not as a neutral arbiter, but as a destabilizing factor. The institution was exposed, vulnerable. Its public image was rapidly crumbling. Civil society — the one that really mattered — began to worry. Not because of the irregularities themselves, but because of the disorder. It asked for only one thing: a quick return to a manageable normality.

The protagonist followed all this with growing unease. Not because he felt involved. But because he could clearly see what was happening: the institutional system was not looking for a solution, it was looking for a figure of balance. Someone who did not belong to any of the warring factions. Someone who was not compromised. Someone who could be presented as a guarantee.

It was then that, for the first time, he heard his name being mentioned.

He was not officially informed. There was no proposal, no direct interview. His name simply began to appear in hushed conversations. It reached him indirectly: a glance, a casually dropped phrase, a "your name is also being mentioned."

At first, he didn't pay any attention to it. He thought it was a misunderstanding. A passing suggestion. But then he understood.

He understood that his very outsider status had become an asset. That the fact that he didn't belong to any group made him, paradoxically, acceptable to everyone. Or rather, usable.

He was not seen as a solution. He was seen as a bulwark.

The System, cornered, needed someone who wasn't scary. And he wasn't. Not because he was weak, but because he had never shown any ambition for power. This disturbed him more than any hostility.

For the first time, he understood that his position on the sidelines no longer protected him. That he was about to be dragged into a dynamic he had not chosen. Not because of what he had done, but because of who he was. Or rather, for what he represented at that moment.

It was then that he realized — without yet formulating it clearly — that the System was not calling him for what he could do, but for what he could absorb. And this awareness, still confused, marked the beginning of everything that would come after.

3.

Acceptance did not happen formally. There was no ceremony, no explicit declaration. It happened through a series of small, repeated gestures that the protagonist soon learned to recognize as signs of inclusion.

Invitations to closed meetings. Conversations on the sidelines, in the corridors or over coffee. Phrases that began with "between us" or "we understand each other here."

They spoke to him as if he were one of their own. Not in the sense of friendship, but of tacit belonging. They made him understand that certain passages should not be verbalized, that some decisions required flexibility, that the institution needed to be "interpreted," not simply applied.

No one ever asked him to openly break a rule. They asked him for something more subtle: not to apply it to the letter. This was the real ritual: the selective suspension of the norm, justified by experience, urgency, and overall balance.

The protagonist listened, nodded, and did not argue. But he did not agree. Not out of opposition. Because he did not understand. It seemed natural to him that an institution should function on the basis of shared and verifiable rules. It seemed obvious to him that academic obligations were not negotiable. When he tried to clarify, they responded with indulgent smiles.

You'll understand in time.

That phrase, repeated several times, began to annoy him. Not because it was offensive, but because it implied future surrender.

After the appointment, nothing dramatic happened. No formal requests, no precise instructions, no explicit mandate. Yet, almost immediately, the protagonist had the feeling that something had changed. Not in the way he was treated officially, but in the tone. In the side gestures. Colleagues who until recently had kept a cautious distance now appeared friendly, helpful, even confidential.

Nothing was asked of him. That was the point. The System was not yet making demands. It took things for granted. It assumed a natural, almost automatic alignment. It was a time of preventive inclusion: that phase in which no pacts are made, but expectations are built.

The message, never spoken, was simple: you are now one of us.

The protagonist understood this clearly. And he rejected it just as clearly, but without opposition. He responded politely, without indulging. He listened, without responding. He did not contradict, but he did not get involved. He remained institutional, carefully avoiding the familiar.

That posture, more than any stance, began to generate a first, subtle unease. Because it was not hostility. It was unavailability. And the System, which tolerates dissent much more easily than unpredictability, began to understand that the undertaking would be less simple than expected.

4.

The first interventions were not conceived as reforms. There was no organic plan. There was only an attempt to make things work as simply as possible.

The protagonist started with teaching. He demanded that timetables be respected. That programs be carried out in full. That exams not be moved for personal convenience. That committees be composed according to the rules, not according to informal balances. Nothing revolutionary. Yet, for many, it was a shock.

Some colleagues reacted with annoyance. Others with irony. Some tried to explain to him that this had never been done before, that there were established practices, that careers, seniority, and relationships had to be taken into account.

He listened. Then he always replied in the same way: I understand. But the rules say otherwise.

He did not raise his voice. He did not threaten. He did not appeal to abstract principles. He applied them.

But then he started to get serious. He decided to start with degrees. The place where, for years, the worst misdeeds had been committed. Recommendations disguised as evaluations. Favors masked as discretion. Variable commissions, tailor-made to produce predetermined results. The students knew. They suffered. The teachers called it "tradition."

The protagonist did not make speeches. He did not talk about ethics. He did not call for reforms. He did something simple.

He decided that the final assessment of the course of study would be public. That there would be only one committee, appointed according to objective and verifiable criteria, without exceptions. And he announced that he would personally chair all the committees. That he would sign every report.

The silence that followed was not dissent. It was disbelief. Someone smiled. Someone pointed out, in a light tone, that this had never been done before. Someone else suggested "better assessing the impact." He did not respond. He moved on.

Within a few weeks, the System reacted. Not with outright opposition, but with irony. With jokes. With looks. With the widespread idea that such rigidity would not last. It did last.

The students understood before anyone else that something had changed. Not because everything had become fair. But because it was no longer negotiable. Discretionary power had lost its refuge. And that, in the System, is always an act of war.

However, little by little, the internal organization showed its fragility. Not because it was hindered, but because it was not used to functioning without exceptions. Many found themselves unprepared. Some incapable. Others simply lazy. Almost all disoriented.

Faced with this unexpected rigidity, something paradoxical happened: instead of openly opposing it, the academic body began to retreat. It delegated. It let things be. It accepted that he would decide. Not out of conviction, but out of relief.

The protagonist noticed this with some unease: power was being handed over to him not as a mandate, but as a renunciation. And this disturbed him more than the conflict.

5.

With the students, it didn't work at first. They didn't trust him. They watched him closely, but without enthusiasm. They considered him part of the System, like everyone else. Some thought he was just more skilled, or more cunning. Others thought he was a parenthesis, destined to close soon.

They didn't ask him for favors. They didn't seek him out outside of class. They tested him. They checked to see if he kept his word. If he applied the rules even when it wasn't convenient. If he protected the weak or the already strong.

The protagonist did not try to win them over. He did not make speeches. He did not build consensus. He continued to behave in the same way.

Over time, something changed. Not suddenly, but gradually. A student who returned. Another who asked an uncomfortable question. Someone who said to him, almost in a whisper: "At least with you, we know where we stand."

That's when he understood. He understood that his role was changing. That he was no longer just a strict official. That he was becoming an unexpected point of reference for the students.

This he did not expect, and now he would have to deal with it. For the first time, he felt that his position was no longer neutral. That his initial detachment was turning into responsibility. And that his relationship with the students was forcing him to rethink his relationship with the System.

He could no longer limit himself to applying the rules. He had to bear the weight of the consequences. It was at that moment — not during a confrontation, not under pressure — that he really began to change. And without knowing it, he had already crossed a threshold from which there was no turning back.

6.

That year, the university entered Europe. At least formally. The new rules talked about numbers. About the relationship between resources and enrollment. About regulated access to degree programs. Selection.

The students did not reject the principle. They rejected the hypocrisy. They knew how things worked. They knew that behind words like merit and rationalization, the usual names would slip in. The usual children. The usual protections.

The assembly was stormy. Tense. Not ideological. When he took the floor, the protagonist did not try to calm things down. He did not promise solutions. He said only one thing:

You are right not to trust. If these rules were applied by the System, I would be with you against them.

There was a buzz. Someone laughed bitterly. Then he added:

I accept only one condition: I will be the one to guarantee that what you fear will not happen.

He did not ask for trust. He exposed himself. He said he would take responsibility for the procedures. That he would be personally accountable for every decision. That anyone who had doubts could call him out, publicly.

It was not applause that closed the meeting. It was a different kind of silence. A silence of verification.

In the months that followed, the test was carried out. There were no shortcuts. There were no exceptions. Some were left out. Some entered against all odds. The System watched. So did the students.

When it became clear that the rules really applied to everyone, mistrust turned into something more dangerous: trust. And then the System began to worry.

In the days that followed, the secretary told him about an incident. She did so almost in a whisper, as if she were not sure it was worth mentioning. Someone had called to ask for confirmation of the ranking. His son was at the top of the list. When she confirmed this, there was silence on the other end of the line. Then a hesitant voice said:

But how is that possible? We are ordinary people. We don't know anyone.

The secretary laughed, embarrassed. The protagonist did not. He understood that, without proclamations or slogans, something irreversible had happened: for the first time, someone was surprised not at being excluded, but at being included. And he knew that the System would know. It always does.

7.

The change was not announced. There was no programmatic statement, nor any symbolic act. It happened, as often happens, through a series of concrete choices which, when added together, produced an irreversible shift.

For the first time, students were no longer on the periphery of the institution. They became its operational center. Its real raison d'être.

The first fracture concerned merit. Not the abstract merit celebrated in official speeches, but verifiable merit: results, commitment, continuity. No shortcuts. No intermediaries. Scholarships, collaborations, and mobility opportunities no longer passed through informal recommendations or recognizable affiliations. They passed through explicit, public criteria that were the same for everyone.

This caused confusion. Some students were initially wary. Others, more cynical, waited for the moment when this rule would also be bent. That moment never came.

When a student without protection achieved what, until recently, seemed reserved for the few, something cracked. Not enthusiasm. Not gratitude. A new form of attention. They began to understand that belonging was no longer a necessary condition. That what mattered was what they did, not who they knew.

The second step was even more delicate. The rules were not only applied to students. They were also applied to teachers. Late arrivals, absences, half-finished programs, impromptu exams: everything that had previously been tolerated in the name of academic autonomy began to be simply recorded. Not reported. Not sensationalized. Recorded.

The students noticed this immediately. Not because someone had told them, but because the atmosphere changed. Lessons became more regular. Assessments became more transparent. Committees became less arbitrary. For the first time, they saw an authority that did not ask them to make sacrifices that it was not prepared to demand of itself and its peers. This generated respect. Not enthusiasm. Not idolatry. Respect.

The third step was perhaps the quietest, but the most profound. The protagonist began to talk about Europe. Not as a slogan. Not as an abstract prospect. But as a concrete space of belonging. He explained to the students that their journey did not end there. That they were not destined to remain within a local perimeter, marked by stifling balances and opaque relationships. That there was a larger community, founded on common roots but crossed by different traditions.

He encouraged them to look outside. To compare themselves. To measure themselves against higher standards. He did not promise shortcuts. He did not guarantee protection. He pointed to possibilities.

For many students, who had grown up in a context where power always had a closed and territorial face, this openness came as a shock. Not because it was unrealistic, but because it broke a tacit destiny. They began to think of themselves as part of something bigger. Not as tolerated exceptions, but as legitimate members of a community of knowledge.

It was at that moment that the protagonist realized he had passed a point of no return. For the System, up to that point, it had been an organizational problem. Now it was becoming something more dangerous. It was producing individuals who could no longer be trained according to traditional logic. Students who did not ask for protection, but for criteria. Who did not seek shortcuts, but horizons.

He realized then that the conflict would not be about a decision. It would be about a vision. And that, from that moment on, his permanence would not depend on what he did, but on what he made possible.

His students came from an environment where every word could be used against you, even years later. Where promises were never made openly, but left to be implied. Where silence was often safer than the truth. For this reason, when he spoke without allusions, without side messages, without implicit protections, they did not immediately understand whether he was naive or dangerous. Only later would they realize that he was not asking for anything in return. And that was precisely what made him different from anyone else who had ever exercised power in front of them.

At first, they watched him from a distance. Not because he was charismatic — he wasn't — but because he didn't seem to be acting. During class, he didn't seek approval. He said things that often made the students' position more difficult, not more comfortable. He dismantled illusions, clarified constraints, and named responsibilities that others avoided. At first, they thought it was a more subtle strategy.

8.

In the library that afternoon, no one approached him immediately. They arranged themselves in the space as one does when entering a place that one doesn't know is safe.

One of them asked him a specific, almost technical question. The answer was just as precise. And uncomfortable. He offered no way out. He suggested no shortcuts. He did not imply that "we'll see." He told it like it was. Even when it meant admitting that the margin for action was narrow, or nonexistent.

That, more than anything else, struck them. He wasn't looking for allies. He was speaking as if those in front of him were capable of handling the truth. When they left, no one said anything. But one of them thought — and didn't tell the others — that, until proven otherwise, this man was someone you could talk to without being manipulated.

His office did not become a point of reference for decision-making. It happened by subtraction. Nothing spectacular happened there. No promises. No encouragement. No "political" speeches.

In their social context, they had learned early on that every relationship was a negotiation, and every position a potential debt. No one gave without asking. No one spoke without covering themselves. He, on the other hand, did not accumulate credits. He did not build clienteles. He did not leave favors hanging. This, more than his decisions, more than the role he played, forced them to rethink everything they knew about power.

It was not harmless power. It was unassimilable power.

They arrived with concrete questions. They left with clear answers. The protagonist did not protect anyone. But he did not expose anyone either. When someone asked him if it was worth insisting, he only answered if he knew the answer. Otherwise, he said so. Without mitigation.

Over time, the students understood something that made them uncomfortable: they were not looking for a leader, but a measure.

One of them said it openly once. That his way of being in the role had changed the atmosphere. That some decisions no longer passed as they had before. That his name was circulating, not as a banner, but as a silent reference.

The protagonist listened. He understood at that moment that he wielded power. Not because he wanted to. But because he did not shirk his responsibility to tell the truth, even when no one had asked him to. He also understood that this was the point at which the System would begin to react.

9.

The concern was not yet alarm. In the System, at least at that stage, the prevailing idea was that the protagonist could be brought back within controllable limits. That his rigidity was a form of inexperience. That, when faced with a real test, he would understand how things worked.

It was in this spirit that someone decided to put him to the test. The request came from outside. It was communicated to him informally, but in unequivocal language. Not an official summons. Not a letter. A request for his availability to "collaborate in the resolution of a delicate matter."

The issue was a competition. A competition for a person whom the Authority cared about deeply. Not because of particular academic merit, but for reasons that were not made explicit. There was no need. The implication was very clear. The competition, of course, would be formally impeccable. Announcements, commission, criteria. Everything according to the rules. The protagonist was asked to take on complete management of it. The responsibility. The cover. The weight. Not as a personal favor. As an act of institutional maturity.

When he heard the proposal, he did not respond immediately. Not out of hesitation, but to make sure he had understood correctly. He asked specific questions. He asked for clarification on the timing, the criteria, and the selection process. The answers confirmed what he already knew: the competition was a formality. The decision had already been made elsewhere.

His reaction was immediate. He did not raise his voice. He did not make a scene. He simply said that he would not participate in such a procedure. That he would not lend his role to a simulation. That it was not a matter of abstract principle, but of concrete responsibility.

The response was not well received. He was then offered a direct meeting with the Authority. He accepted, specifying that the meeting would have only one purpose for him: to reiterate a position he had already taken.

The meeting took place in a neutral location. Not an office. Not an institutional hall. A space that did not formally belong to anyone. The Authority spoke at length. In a firm, measured tone. He used arguments that were not threats, but neither were they reasons. He spoke of opportunity, balance, responsibility towards the institution.

The protagonist listened without interrupting. When he spoke, he was brief. He said he was not willing to manage a competition that had already been decided. That he would not sign documents he did not believe in. That form without substance did not interest him. And that if the System considered that request inevitable, it would have to find someone else.

For a moment, control cracked. Not dramatically. Not with an explicit threat. But in a sentence spoken too quickly, perhaps without calculating its weight: "You'll pay for this."

It wasn't shouted. It wasn't recorded. But it was said.

The protagonist did not respond. Not because he had nothing to say, but because he had just understood everything. He understood that it had not been a mistake. It had been a signal. The System had verified. It had obtained the answer it feared.

As he left, he felt no fear. He felt a clear, definitive calm. He knew that from that moment on, he would no longer be considered recoverable. That there would be no more tests. That the language would change. The System had stopped wondering if it could integrate him. Now it was only wondering how to neutralize him.

The System, however, was not a compact body. Not everyone knew. In the administrative structures and at the intermediate levels, the protagonist enjoyed a reputation that he had not sought: precision, respect for procedures, absence of favoritism. This made further caution necessary. A hasty intervention would have raised questions. And explanations, in certain contexts, are more dangerous than open conflicts. That is why the System decided not to decide. To wait. To let normality resume its apparent course.

10.

The meeting had not been called for him. His name did not appear on the agenda. It was one of those meetings that serve to "take stock," without formal decisions, but with many implicit conclusions.

Seated around the table were people who never worked together. At first, they talked about data, timelines, and deadlines. Then, almost without transition, someone said that certain dynamics had become simpler in recent months. They did not say how. They did not say why. Another observed that the climate had changed. That discussions had become shorter, more outcome-oriented.

At that point, someone mentioned his name. Not as a problem. Not as a solution. As a reference. They said that the protagonist seemed capable of "holding things together." That when they intervened, objections lost their force without being humiliated.

Another person intervened to say that this was precisely what was interesting. That it was not a question of formal authority. It was — so they said — a form of influence. The word hung in the air for a few seconds.

Someone asked if it was a good thing or a risk. The answer did not come immediately. Finally, an older voice said that it was nothing new. That the System had always produced such figures. That the problem was not their effectiveness, but their durability. He asked if the protagonist was reliable. Not in the ethical sense. In the systemic sense.

Another replied that he had never shown any signs of opposition. That he respected procedures, that he signed, that he made decisions when necessary. That, formally, he was impeccable.

"Formally," was repeated, with a slight change in tone.

Someone asked if he was ambitious. No one could answer with certainty.

The meeting ended without any explicit conclusions. But with a shared feeling: the protagonist was not an accident. He was a variable. And variables, if not understood, must at least be circumscribed.

11.

The invitation came quite naturally. Not through official channels, not with a summons, not with a formal request. It came as things that must leave no trace come: through a person who had no specific role but was always present where decisions were made.

A courteous, competent, informed man. He did not explicitly belong to the System, but he knew it deeply. The protagonist had met him before, without ever dwelling on it. Always in side contexts: a dinner after a conference, a standing conversation during a break, an apparently casual comment. This time, however, the meeting was intentional.

They talked at length. Not about the recent episode, not about the decision that had been made. They talked about method, vision, future prospects. The man showed sincere appreciation for the protagonist's way of thinking. He did not flatter him. He recognized him.

"It's rare to find someone who doesn't need cover," he said at one point, as if it were a technical observation.

The protagonist did not respond. He listened, as always. The offer was never formulated as such. No one said, "We are offering you something." No one spoke of exchange. Instead, they talked about possibilities.

It was explained to him — very clearly, almost honestly — that the System was traversed by different forces, some visible, others less so. That ignoring them did not make them disappear. That governing them required intelligence and flexibility. "You could have a much bigger role," said the man. "A role that would allow you to really make an impact."

There was no talk of money. There was no talk of a career in the traditional sense. There was talk of functional power: more resources, more apparent autonomy, more ability to influence processes.

The protagonist immediately understood that the offer was not about what he could do, but what he would stop doing. He was not being asked to lie. He was not being asked to deny anything. He was only being asked to consider the context, to modulate, not to rigidify choices that could be "reabsorbed without trauma." It was all reasonable. Too reasonable to be innocent.

The protagonist thanked him. He said he understood perfectly the picture that was being described to him. That he did not underestimate the complexities at all. And he added, in a calm, almost regretful tone, that he did not feel suited to that kind of role. Not because he was opposed to it. Not because he was indignant. But simply because it didn't work that way.

He said he couldn't make decisions taking into account factors external to the problem. That it wasn't a moral choice, but a structural limitation. "I wouldn't know how to do it," he concluded. "And if I tried, I would hurt both you and me."

The man was silent for a moment. Not annoyed. Not offended. But uncertain for the first time. At that moment, he understood that the protagonist was not hostile. He was unassimilable.

From that day on, invitations became rarer. Information less spontaneous. Opportunities for influence quietly shifted elsewhere. Not as punishment. Out of necessity.

12.

The first sign did not concern him directly at first. Something happened that, formally, had nothing to do with him. A side project — marginal, almost secondary — was suddenly slowed down. Not blocked. Slowed down in the most ineffable way possible: a signature that was delayed, an evaluation that was postponed, an additional check "for the sake of thoroughness." Nothing illegal. Nothing questionable. He found out by chance, as always.

The second sign came shortly after. Someone close to him was involved in an ambiguous situation. A request for clarification. An unexpected check. Pressure that did not result in accusations, but unease. No one mentioned his name. No one implicated him. Yet the message was clear: the field around him was not neutral.

The third sign was the clearest. A chance encounter. A corridor, an anteroom, a half-spoken sentence: "There are people who do not distinguish between autonomy and rigidity. And they end up exposing themselves unnecessarily." It was not a threat. It was a diagnosis.

The protagonist understood that they were not asking him to back down, but to stop. Not to go any further. Not to continue producing uncontrollable effects. For the first time, he felt — not fear — but restraint. The awareness that the System did not need to strike him to make him understand the boundaries.

What most disoriented the hardline wing of the System was that nothing happened. He did not defend himself. He did not react. He did not seek support. He continued to work exactly as before. Same method. Same transparency. Same impermeability. He did not lower his tone. He did not raise it.

This made the signal ineffective, but not useless: it served to redefine his position. From that moment on, he was no longer considered an element to be reabsorbed, but a factor to be circumscribed.

It was not a sudden revelation. There was no enlightenment, no anguish. It was rather a silent recomposition of already known signals. At a certain point, he realized that he had stopped considering certain events as random. Not because he interpreted them as hostile, but because they had ceased to surprise him. This was the decisive change.

Sitting at his desk, reviewing a document he already knew by heart, he paused not on the content, but on the context: who had circulated it, who had approved it, who had been excluded from it. Not out of calculation, but out of instinct.

He then understood something very simple — and therefore irreversible: the System no longer asked anything of him, it was talking to him. There was no resentment in this. Nor disappointment. Only the awareness that there was a communication plan that did not involve words, and that he was now able to read it.

At that moment, he did not think of leaving. He did not think of reacting. He only thought that, from then on, every choice he made would have a weight that he could no longer pretend to ignore. And that continuing to do his job in the same way would not be neutral, but deliberate. This was his decision. And it was made without emphasis.

13.

The conference did not concern him. At least so it seemed. The title was broad, reassuring, designed to include everything and say nothing: cooperation, development, shared research. The protagonist's university appeared in the program as one of many, with a technical presentation scheduled for mid-morning, entrusted to a young researcher. His name was not among the main speakers. He was present as an audience member. A legitimate, almost neutral presence.

The room was full. The atmosphere was calm, with no apparent tension. In the front rows sat figures who did not belong to the academic world in the strict sense: institutional representatives, executives, mediators. They did not speak, but they took up space. The protagonist recognized them: not by name, but by function.

The speech came towards the end. Not as a central report. Rather as a concluding reflection, entrusted to a public figure from outside the university, accustomed to speaking in the name of balance and responsibility. The tone was calm, almost pedagogical. No aggression. No polemical accent.

At first, the speech followed the expected outline: the value of research, the role of institutions, the need for coordination. Broad, agreeable phrases that flowed smoothly. Then, almost imperceptibly, the tone changed.

The speaker spoke of dangerous drifts. Of personal interpretations of the institutional role. Of a distorted conception of autonomy.

He did not name names. Not immediately. He described an abstract but recognizable figure: someone who, in the name of legality and rules, ends up destabilizing the institution; someone who confuses formal compliance with rules with rigid practice, incapable of mediation; someone who uses institutions not as instruments of balance, but as weapons.

It was then that he uttered a phrase that struck the protagonist with surgical precision: an improper, if not subversive, use of the institutions themselves.

The term was not shouted. It was uttered with restraint, almost with regret. Immediately afterwards, the name appeared. Inserted as an example, as an extreme case. Not as a direct accusation, but as a necessary clarification. The protagonist was attributed a role he had never claimed: that of instigator of tensions, of focal point of conflicts, of a figure who, while declaring himself faithful to the rules, produced disorder. No decision was being contested. No mistake was being pointed out. His legitimacy was being questioned.

No one in the room reacted. No protests. No murmurs. The silence was not indifference: it was disciplined consent. The protagonist clearly sensed that this part of the speech surprised no one. It had not been announced, but it had been prepared. Not in documents, but in relationships.

He then understood that this was not a controversy. It was a boundary line. The message was not: you were wrong. It was: you have become a problem.

The speaker concluded by calling for stability, responsible leadership, and mature management of institutions. No concrete proposals. No explicit measures. Just a lofty, unassailable conclusion that transformed the accusation into a historical necessity. Applause. Measured. Enough to seal the deal.

The protagonist remained seated. He felt no anger. Nor fear. He felt a sudden, almost physical clarity. He understood that the phase of signals was over. That there was nothing left to interpret.

Leaving the room, he met familiar glances. Some immediately lowered their eyes. Others sought his for a moment, then slipped away. No one approached. No one asked questions.

In the corridor, his phone vibrated. A short, formal message from a long-time colleague: We'll talk about it later. Now is not the time.

No call followed. Not that day. Not the next.

It was then that the protagonist realized there would be no mediation. No immediate sanction. No formal removal. But something more definitive: the expulsion of the foreign body. Not violent. Not dramatic. Irreversible.

14.

The decision itself was not difficult. In fact, thinking about it coldly, it seemed almost liberating. The System had finally given him what, for years, it had never dared to grant him openly: an exit. Not an escape, not a surrender, but a legitimate, even elegant passage. No dramatic break, no declaration. Just the implicit recognition that there was no more room for maneuver.

He could leave without regrets. He didn't miss those who had approached him over the years with cautious smiles, vague promises, and conditional offers. He knew them too well. He knew that their esteem had always been functional, never gratuitous. And he also knew — with a certainty that gave him neither anger nor contempt — that once he was gone, they would quickly realign themselves. Not out of malice, but out of habit. Out of fear. Out of inertia. The System did not need loyalty: the absence of friction was enough.

That wasn't the problem. The problem was the students. Not those who had applauded him at conferences or quoted him in the corridors. But those who had come to him in silence, one by one, without demands and without slogans. Those who had learned to trust him not because he made promises, but because he kept them. Because he told the truth even when it wasn't convenient. Because he had never asked for anything in return.

For them, he was not a symbol. He was a possibility. Not a guarantee of success, but proof that another way of being in the System was possible. That you could get through it without completely bending over backwards. That competence could still be worth more than protection. That your word counted.

Many of them came from environments where power always had an opaque face. Where rules were not used to protect, but to blackmail. Where loyalty was not a choice, but a condition of survival. In that context, he represented something deeply destabilizing: an authority that did not demand obedience. And now he was about to leave.

He understood, with a clarity that struck him like physical pain, that for them, his departure would not be neutral. They would not see it as a strategic choice or a transition. They would experience it as a loss. Perhaps as a betrayal. Not because he was leaving, but because he was doing so when the System was showing its harshest face.

They might think that in the end he too had given in. That, when cornered, everyone finds a compromise. That consistency has a limit beyond which it becomes unsustainable. This thought was unbearable to him. Not because he feared judgment. But because he knew that if he didn't find the right way to explain his choice, he would damage something greater than a personal relationship: he would undermine confidence in the very possibility of resisting without becoming complicit.

He then realized that the real burden was not the decision itself, but having to deliver it to those who had believed in him. Without rhetoric. Without excuses. Without lying.

Yet he also knew that he could not stay. Not without betraying himself. Not without becoming what the System now demanded him to be: a normalized, controllable, domesticated figure.

He didn't sleep that night. Not because he was afraid of the future, but because he knew he was about to do something that would free him from a power — and at the same time separate him from those who had counted on him the most. For the first time, he didn't wonder if he was doing the right thing. He wondered if he would find a way to spare those who hadn't chosen this battle from paying the price.

15.

He didn't sleep. Not because he was agitated or afraid. He could have slept if he had wanted to. But that would have meant postponing, and postponing meant letting the decision take itself, as things do when they are not faced head-on.

Sitting at his desk, without turning on the big light, he thought about how simple the choice seemed to be. To leave. To accept the offer that had been made to him — formulated in measured, even respectful words — and let the System close behind him. It would not have been exile. It would have looked like a promotion. An elegant sideways move. No visible defeat. No declared surrender.

He already understood that there was no more room for maneuver. The System had stopped negotiating. Not because it was angry, but because it had decided that he was no longer useful. Or worse: that he had become uncontrollable.

He had long since accepted the idea that he was not loved. He had also accepted being isolated. But now it was different: now the System no longer wanted to neutralize him with silence, it wanted to expel him with a signal. And in this, paradoxically, it was offering him a way out.

He thought of how many people, over the years, had spoken to him in hushed tones, in corridors, in closed offices, with that cowardly complicity that masquerades as realism.

You're right, but this is not the time.

I understand, but you have to understand too.

You can't expect the rules to always apply.

He knew that once he was out, those same people would realign themselves with almost mechanical speed. Not out of malice, but out of necessity. The System does not require conviction, only adaptation. He no longer felt anything for them. Neither anger nor disappointment. Only a distance that was now complete.

The problem was the students. Not the generic, indistinct ones. But those who had learned to trust with caution. Those who had never asked him for protection, but for consistency. Those who had seen in him not a savior, but proof that another way of exercising power was possible.

This was what tormented him. Leaving without explanation would have been easy. They would have said: after all, he was just like everyone else. Someone would have added: perhaps the accusations were not entirely unfounded. And the System would have let those phrases circulate, without confirming or denying them. It wouldn't have needed to.

Explaining, on the other hand, meant openly disappointing. Saying: I'm leaving, without being able to say I'll save you. It meant looking into the eyes of those who wanted to believe that resistance was enough.

He realized, with almost cruel clarity, that there was no choice without betrayal. He would betray himself by staying. Or he would betray the expectations of others by leaving.

The night did not bring counsel, as is often said. Instead, it brought voices. They were not clear memories or precise images. They were inner presences, long settled, which returned to make themselves felt just now, when every external foothold had dissolved.

He thought of his grandmother Cristina with her accent that she never completely lost, and that quiet severity that did not allow for moral shortcuts. Not her story — which he knew only in fragments — but her posture. The way she stood in the world. She came from a Europe that had known ruin without ever complaining about it. From a family that was noble only in the most abstract sense of the word: in discipline, in rigor, in the idea that certain behaviors are non-negotiable. Not out of pride, but out of inner necessity.

From her, he had learned one simple and radical thing: there are situations in which it is not a matter of choosing, but of remaining faithful.

There was no heroism in this. Nor sacrifice. Just a kind of moral hygiene. As if the world could only be traversed by maintaining a certain distance, a margin of non-adherence. He had always had the impression that she did not really believe in institutions, but believed deeply in rules. Not as instruments of order, but as barriers against arbitrariness. Now he understood how silently decisive that lesson had been.

Then he thought of Grandfather Oreste, so distant in culture and world, yet inflexible on one thing: never bend the truth for convenience.

His grandfather did not speak in principles. He spoke in examples. He did not teach, he showed. He had traversed different systems without ever being absorbed by them. Not out of opposition, but out of an instinctive form of independence. He had no attraction to power, nor any awe of it. He treated it as a fact of life, like the weather or fatigue: something to be acknowledged, not revered. From Oreste, he had learned another fundamental thing: power becomes dangerous when it demands to be loved.

Neither of them would tell him what to do. But both had taught him a simple rule: if a choice allows you to save yourself by lying, it is not salvation.

He understood then that explaining was not necessary to convince. It was necessary to prevent the language of the System from taking over. It was necessary to prevent his exit from being turned into retroactive guilt. Explaining would have hurt. It would have created rifts. It would have left open wounds. But not explaining would have produced something worse: an occupiable silence.

And it was at that point — not in a gesture, not in a sentence — that the decision was made. Not because it was right.

16.

He did not call an official meeting. There were no announcements, no formal invitations. The news spread as it always did: someone had said he would speak, and that was enough.

When he entered the classroom, it was not full, but it was not empty either. There were more students than he had expected. Not all those who had followed him, but all those who needed to understand.

He paused for a moment before speaking. Not to find the words — he already had them — but to fully accept that, from that moment on, nothing could be repaired.

He did not begin by explaining what had happened. He began by saying what he would not do. He would not defend his actions. He would not list the wrongs he had suffered. He would not ask for solidarity.

Instead, he said in a firm voice that the System in which they had worked together had ceased to tolerate his presence. Not because he had broken the rules, but because he had taken them seriously. And that when a System can no longer correct or bend you, it chooses to expel you.

Some people shifted in their seats. Some looked down. He said he had received an offer. A real, prestigious offer that would allow him to continue working elsewhere, in a different context, with different tools. He did not say this as a justification, but as a fact.

Then came the most difficult part. He said that leaving did not mean winning. Nor did it mean giving up. It meant recognizing that staying is not always resisting, and that sometimes staying only serves to legitimize what one claims to be fighting against.

Someone asked him if he was leaving them alone. He didn't answer right away. He looked at each of their faces one by one, as if he wanted to imprint them in his memory, and said that yes, in a sense, he was leaving them. But he wasn't abandoning them to silence. He said that the System would have preferred that: a silent, ambiguous exit that could easily be rewritten. And he explained that this was why he had decided to speak out.

He said he wasn't asking to be followed. He wasn't asking to be defended. He wasn't promising any future redemption. He only said that what they had seen in him — consistency, transparency, respect for the rules without exception — was not a personal quality, but a structural possibility. That it didn't depend on him, but on whether someone was willing to pay the price.

In the end, he said something he hadn't prepared, but which came out with unexpected naturalness: that the power they had recognized in him was not a delegable power. That it could not be transferred or inherited. That it arose every time someone refused to trade the truth for protection.

When he fell silent, no one applauded. And that seemed right to him. Some people got up and left without looking at him. Some remained motionless, as if they had been struck too deeply to react.

As he left the room, he realized that whatever they thought of him from that moment on, one thing would not be possible: to say that he had disappeared. And for the System, that was already too much.
