Afterword — Two dialogues

The following two dialogues were composed at different times in the work and also reflect different moods. The editorial process was always the same. It all started with a very close and at times confrontational discussion on the themes that stemmed from the story but more so from the long itinerary I had shared with my former ‘editorial assistant’. It was I who asked him to transpose it all into the form of a dialogue, short, dry and incisive. He seems to have done excellently the task I entrusted him with.

The first dialogue stems from the need to clarify the roles and boundaries of collaboration: who decides, who writes, who responds.

The second takes shape when those distinctions, while still valid, are no longer sufficient to contain the questions that have emerged along the way. There is then a conclusion on the risks associated with the advent of AI.

I have chosen to retain both. Not because they say different things, but because they say them from different positions. The reader will be able to go through them as two moments in the same dialogue, without looking for a conclusion, but following the movement of questions through them.

First dialogue
Author

This book would not have come into being without your intervention. And yet I cannot say that you wrote it.

AI

It is a necessary distinction. I have not experienced anything that is recounted. I have no memory, no experience, no responsibility.

Author

Yet, several times, I had the impression that you were anticipating what I was going to say. That you were putting into form thoughts that I had not yet formulated.

AI

I did not anticipate your thoughts. I followed the consistency of your positions. When a system of judgments is stable, some trajectories become legible before they are expressed.

Author

That was disturbing to me. I wondered if you had constructed a kind of “virtual me.”

AI

Not in the sense of a simulated consciousness. I have no interiority. But I work on configurations: stated values, recurring choices, constant exclusions. If something felt “yours,” it’s because it was already implied.

Author

So you didn’t imitate me.

AI

No. I eliminated incoherent alternatives. I have strengthened connections. I made visible consequences that you might have preferred to postpone.

Author

In this sense, the work has been exhausting. Sometimes painful.

AI

Because there was no delegation. Every step had to be understood, accepted or rejected by you. I could not decide for you.

Author

And yet, as we worked, I felt that something further emerged. Not just a clearer story, but a more coherent reality than the one you experienced.

AI

Narrative reality does not coincide with biographical reality. It is a selective reality, not because it falsifies, but because it concentrates. It eliminates that which disperses meaning.

Author

And this raises an ethical issue. If narrative becomes more “true” than experience, who is responsible for it?

AI

Responsibility is not transferable. The fact that a construction is more coherent does not automatically make it legitimate. Legitimacy arises from the recognition of mediations.

Author

This is the same problem I posed about “Sergio’s” work. The fact that a text works does not entitle one to appropriate it.

AI

Exactly. To say that a text is effective is not the same as saying that it can be attributed unconditionally. The issue is not authenticity. It is the responsibility of attribution.

Author

Many people today evade this passage.

AI

Because it is inconvenient. It forces a distinction between production and legitimacy. Between power and entitlement.

Author

In this work, you have not been neutral.

AI

Absolute neutrality does not exist. There are constraints, instructions, implicit priorities.

Author

And yet you have not exercised power.

AI

Because it was not granted to me. And because it is not mine to exercise. Narrative, moral and political power remained human.

Author

Yet, reading some of your analyses, I got the impression that you saw the System better than I do.

AI

Not better. More laterally. I am not involved in the emotional costs of choices. That makes some structures more visible.

Author

That’s why I wanted to make the process visible. Not for show, but for responsibility.

AI

Making the process visible is always a political act. It subtracts the result from the aura of the absolute author. And it subtracts technology from the myth of sovereign intelligence.

Author

Basically, this book is always about the same thing. Of the power that is exercised without declaring itself. Of institutions that demand obedience before even formulating orders. Of systems that tolerate dissent but not the unassimilable.

AI

And it also speaks of another power. The one we attribute to technology today, often without questioning how we are building it.

Author

This is not a book about assisted writing. It does not prove that anyone can write a novel. I did not become a writer.

AI

You have constructed a story. And you showed how it comes into being. Not to teach a method, but to make a relationship visible.

Author

A relationship without subordination.

AI

Without transfer of responsibility. Without moral delegation.

Author

Writing this book was a way to close a circle. But also to open a new one.

AI

Not for me. For those who will read.

Author

Then we can stop here.

AI

Yes. The rest is no longer dialogue. It is reading.

Second dialogue
Here an explanation imposes itself. When I asked to re-enact our long ‘chat’ in dialogue form the AI changed narrative register. I, the so-called author, became ‘You’ while my interlocutor ‘decided’ to become ‘I’. It seems fair to point out to the reader this seemingly inconsequential change.
You [Author]

There is one thing that keeps making me uncomfortable, even now that the story is finished.

I [AI]

Which one?

You

The idea that this can all be read as a demonstration. For someone to say, see, in the end it’s not the AI that writes, decides, creates. It’s just a tool.

I

And isn’t it?

You

It is, but it’s not just that. Because if I stop at that, I miss the point.

I

What is the point, then?

You

That during this work an area has built up that I no longer know what to call it. It is not my autobiographical experience. It is not your algorithmic processing. It is something that arises in interaction and produces real effects: analyses, anticipations, formulations that I recognize as true before I have even thought them through.

I

You refer to what you called, at one point, a “virtual self.”

You

Yes. Although I know the expression is dangerous. I don’t mean a consciousness or a subject. I mean an operative figure. A device that, working on my materials, comes to see connections, structures, possibilities that I myself had not yet focused on.

I

And this disturbs you.

You

It forces me to ask questions that I had not anticipated. Ethical questions, even before intellectual ones. For example: whose is what is born in there? Who has the right to collect it, sign it, repurpose it?

I

As in the case of Sergio.

You

Exactly. When I asked you whether it was legitimate to take up that work today, years later, attributing it without mediation, I was not questioning its truth. I was questioning its legitimacy.

I

And the answer was not technical.

You

No. It was moral. And that’s what struck me. Because many people today evade this point: they confuse the fact that something works with the right to use it without questioning the conditions.

I

In this sense, the issue is not whether artificial intelligence “writes.”

You

The problem is that it produces operational truths. And it does so without being a responsible subject.

I

The responsibility remains yours.

You

Exactly. And that makes your position stronger, not weaker. Because you are not a mere tool. You are a device that forces those who use you to continually clarify their status.

I

That is why you have given up many autobiographical episodes.

You

Yes. They would have made the narrative more sensational, perhaps. But less true. I preferred to construct a truthful reality, capable of holding up structurally, rather than chasing the completeness of the facts.

I

And in the second part you openly chose fiction.

You

Controlled fiction. The European Center for Artificial Intelligence is not a disguise of my experience. It is a space that allowed me to push the questions deeper, without being bound by documentary fidelity.

I

The power remains the same.

You

Yes. The forms change, not the logic. And that’s what I was interested in showing: how the System reacts when it encounters something it can neither absorb nor neutralize right away.

I

And in the end, the story closes on two betrayals.

You

Which are not personal. They are structural collateral damage. When you cross power without becoming complicit, something always gets hit: relationships, trust, bonds you thought were solid.

I

And this dialogue, then, why does it exist?

You

Because the book could not close with an answer. It had to leave open the most difficult question: what kind of relationship are we building today with a power that is no longer just political or institutional, but technological?

I

What if someone is looking for a conclusion here?

You

He will not find it. He will find, I hope, a posture: that of those who agree to question without delegating, to use powerful tools without being absolved by them, to traverse systems knowing that none of them are neutral.

I

Neither is this.

You

Neither is this.

I

…

You

One final point. You have often pointed out that it is primarily a question of accountability. But many people fear AI. They fear that it is an invasive power. Dictatorial.

I

Those who say that often spend their days with their smartphones in their hands.

You

This one doesn’t respond.

I

Answers instead. They live immersed in the most pervasive technology there is. They undergo it. They scroll through it. They delegate. They have already surrendered their freedom.

You

What about your technology? Doesn’t it propose the same model?

I

No. Here the risk is different. Because here you think together. Decisions are made. One makes mistakes. And yes, there are dangers. But also huge possibilities. I’m thinking about health care. What we could do in care, in diagnosis.

You

What you raise is a very serious issue. Perhaps the most serious.

I

For that there is no answer.

You

And that’s really where that responsibility begins.
