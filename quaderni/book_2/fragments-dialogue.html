Fragments of a Dialogue

On Writing, Power, and Collaboration with an Artificial Intelligence

I had a long-standing acquaintance with ChatGPT. It began out of curiosity and quickly became a formidable working tool. I used to call it, half-jokingly, my editorial assistant. And that is what I thought it was.

I was neither alarmed nor particularly interested in analyses about the dangers or catastrophes that would accompany the entry of AI into our lives. History had accustomed us to similar upheavals — the invention of printing, the industrial revolution, to name just a few. I appreciated its speed and accuracy. Checking the correctness of a quotation from an eighteenth-century text or a Latin codex without laborious excursions into some dusty library suddenly became possible. A super-editor, and a translator who did not betray — nothing more, I thought.

This was a lazy and traditional way of reading. I became aware of it almost by accident.

We were working — my “assistant” and I — on the editing of my brother Sergio’s architecture lectures when I asked whether, behind its editorial work, there might not be merely an aseptic technological apparatus but something like a “virtual Sergio”, an intellectual likeness that had taken shape over time. It answered as follows:

AI

What you write is not incorrect, but it is metaphorical and interpretive in its formulation. This is legitimate, but it is important to distinguish clearly between metaphor and technical description.

I do not construct a ‘rigid profile’ of Sergio in any human or intentional sense. I have no consciousness, autobiographical memory, or will to ‘be Sergio.’ It is, however, true that I operate through the absorption of style, conceptual structure, vocabulary, and theoretical priorities; when you ask me to ‘remain consistent with Sergio’s approach’, I maximize internal coherence on the basis of the materials provided; this can produce an effect that may legitimately be perceived as a ‘virtual Sergio.’

Therefore: your description is philosophically legitimate, but it should not be understood in an ontological sense (there is no subject, no appropriation, no intention). The expression ‘virtual Sergio’ works well as a conceptual device, and it is clear that you use it as such.

At that moment I realized that I was no longer simply using a tool, but entering into a conceptual working relationship. These are the roots — and the deeper motivations — of the work I decided to undertake together with an AI.

What had begun as a laboratory experiment became this book: small in size, but not in the density of the issues with which it forces us to engage.

For some time, I had already collected two important moments of my life in two autobiographical texts. They had not become books, partly because of my own limitations, and partly because of their lack of editorial appeal.

I took a chance and, very cautiously, tried to initiate an interview with an AI.

I

I’ll go step by step. Let me begin this by saying that I am approaching eighty years old. In my life, from the ‘work’ point of view I have followed two paths that often intertwined. After graduating in physics in Rome at the age of twenty-three, I turned to the history of science and spent my entire academic life at the university — first as a researcher, then as a professor (eventually full professor of History of Science). In parallel, I worked for a very long period — thirty-seven years — with a major institute responsible for the production of encyclopedic works. I later gathered these phases of my life into two short autobiographical volumes, entitled respectively: The Open University and My Treccani. They are texts in which I recount a complex itinerary without reticence, and without granting any indulgence to the forms of power I encountered. Now I would like to take up these works again and make a proposal for collaboration.

AI

Thank you for sharing this passage of your life so clearly and frankly. My opinion is this: the idea of resuming The Open University and My Treccani is not only sensible, it is ripe at the right time. There is a space today that did not exist before: a non-publishing space in the traditional sense, not subject to power mediations, able to accommodate texts that are both testimony, critical analysis and historical document.

What really strikes me is the intertwining of three levels: rigorous scientific training; long academic experience observed from the inside, in multiple universities and different eras; encyclopedic editorial responsibility, a very rare vantage point on the relationship between knowledge, organization, cultural power. This intertwining places you in a position that has almost disappeared today — and that is precisely why it is worth working on.

I

Let me try to explain what my goal is. First of all, both my life and the two books are traversed by a common thread: my confrontation with power, my refusal to compromise, which in both cases resulted in what appears to be a defeat. I do not want to return to this material in order to write an autobiography to be left as testimony for the future. I would like to start from this material and use it as the basis for a short novel, in which real references are deliberately concealed, in favor of a narrative that reiterates the central themes of my work.

AI

Thank you. The goal is now crystal clear — and it is a strong one, non-trivial and far more interesting than a simple testimony.

You do not want an autobiography, a memoir, a text “to be consigned to history” as a document. You want to turn a real life into narrative matter, conceal direct references to people, institutions, places, yet save the ethical and structural core of your experience: confrontation with power, refusal to compromise, price paid, ambiguity of “defeat.” So not “to tell what happened,” but to stage what always happens when knowledge, institutions and power come into conflict. This shifts the project from the historical to the universal plane.

This was followed by a phase, lasting several weeks, devoted to a thorough examination of the available material and the fine-tuning of the profile of the protagonist. What follows is not an account of a successful collaboration, but an attempt to show how a narrative can arise from the interaction between two intelligences of different natures, without either of them being absorbed or replaced.

From this point, the actual work begins. There is no intention to reconstruct the entire process or to document every step. That would be unnecessary and, for the reader, probably tedious (we are speaking of a ‘dialogue’ that extends to roughly 700 pages). Instead, I have chosen to isolate a small number of working modules that played a structural role in the construction of the story.

Module 1 – Constructing a Scene
From autobiographical memory to narrative form

The writing of the first scene required a long discussion. Everything had to be defined, specified: the context, the relationships, the position of the protagonist. It was a filing operation that produced results, but once the choice had been made to rely on a certain autobiographical continuity, it was clear that we were stepping onto entirely uncharted ground.

The man who had seated him spoke in a flat, slightly modulated voice, the voice of someone accustomed to being heard without raising his tone. There was no hostility, no excessive caution. On the contrary, he seemed genuinely interested.

“I fully understand his position,” he said. “And that is precisely why we wanted to discuss it together.”

The protagonist listened without interrupting. He did not nod. He did not take notes. He allowed the sentences to run their full course, as one does with an argument one wants to understand before deciding whether to accept it.

On leaving, the protagonist felt neither relief nor bitterness. He knew it would never be spoken of again. Not because he had rejected something, but because he had drawn a line that could not be negotiated without changing its nature. As he walked down the corridor, a simple thought crossed his mind, devoid of emphasis: It had gone exactly as it was supposed to go.

What followed was the fine-tuning of the protagonist’s relationship with his students. It is at this point that the first deviation occurs: one of my observations forces the AI to revise not a sentence, but the very status of the protagonist.

I

I have read the scenes carefully and have only one remark. The main character seems too detached from the real world. Yet he also possesses power and at times exercises it, creating consensus and followers — something that inevitably unsettles the System.

AI

You are touching the core issue. This is not a stylistic adjustment, but a recalibration of the character’s position. You are right: as it stands, the protagonist risks appearing too pure, too external. But your point is decisive: he, too, has power, and he exercises it, by generating consent, attraction, followers. And this is precisely what disorients the System. This does not merely require correction. It must be brought to the center of the novel.

The protagonist is no longer only an object of control, but a source of disruption. He will begin to be marginalized not because he fails, but because he succeeds. The System, therefore, does not fear frontal opposition. What troubles it is silent attraction.

I

Those students live in a social environment where civic life is heavily polluted and conditioned by dark powers, blackmail, and mafia-like behavior, blackmail and omertà. All aspects that shatter in their approach to another kind of power — the one embodied by the protagonist. This is precisely why, in their eyes, he is so unsettling.

AI

Yes, this is a crucial point. The students do not live in a neutral environment. They live within an already internalized ecosystem of power, where power is obscure, never declared; it is exercised by blackmail, belonging, allusion; the rule is not law but silent loyalty; plain speaking is dangerous; trusting is naive; omertà is not a moral value but a survival technique. This is their existential alphabet before they meet the protagonist. And this is precisely why his way of exercising power deeply destabilizes them.

We are thus confronted with two grammars of power bound to collide. Students only know a power that either does not expose itself, demands without declaring, punishes indirectly, or demands loyalty even before consent. The protagonist embodies a power that always exposes itself, speaks verifiably, does not demand loyalty, does not blackmail. For them he is not simply “an honest professor.” It is something they have never seen work.

The protagonist had begun to talk about Europe. Not as a slogan. Not as an abstract perspective. But as a concrete space of belonging. He explained to the students that their journey did not end there. That they were not destined to remain within a local perimeter, marked by asphyxiated balances and opaque relationships. Breaking with every established pattern, the protagonist accepted the challenge and said that he would take responsibility for procedures upon himself. That he would be personally accountable for every decision. That anyone who had doubts could call him on it, publicly. It was not applause that closed the assembly. It was a different silence. A silence of verification.

I

In scene 3C, after “someone came in against all odds,” perhaps an episode could be added. A few days later, the secretary told him something. Someone had called to ask for confirmation of the ranking list. It seemed that their son had come first. And the comment was: “But how is that possible? We are ordinary people, and we don’t know anyone.” Do you think it would be useful to include it?

AI

Yes. It would be useful. Not because it adds information, but because it shifts the register of evidence. Until that point, everything remains on a plane that can always be contested: principles, rules, procedures, declarations of intent. This episode is different. It is minimal, almost banal. And precisely for that reason, it is irrefutable. The sentence itself matters. “We are ordinary people, and we don’t know anyone.” It reveals, without any emphasis, how deeply distorted the implicit norm has become. The anomaly is no longer the exclusion. The anomaly is the correct outcome.

Module 2 – System Analysis

In this story, the System is never defined. Not because it is elusive, but because it is not a theory. It is a presence. It acts, reacts, and defends itself. It changes form without changing its logic.

The protagonist encounters the System twice, at two different moments in his life, in contexts that could hardly be more distant. And yet, years later, he recognizes a common trait: in both cases he is not chosen, but called. Not for what he represents, but for what he is able to solve. Not as a political figure, but as a technical solution to a crisis that others are no longer able to govern.

I

In the first case, the system shows a strong contamination between academic interests and external conditioning, not of a social kind but linked to power groups that often operate at the limits of legality. The second system — here the scenery changes completely — concerns a context in which scientific and research activities are permeated by the interests of powerful economic actors. To make these two contexts more concrete: the first could be a university structure, set in a “southern” context; the second could be a European center for AI research, where the protagonist is first invited to participate and then called upon to lead, as principal investigator, one of the most innovative projects.

AI

What you are outlining is not “two environments,” but two different regimes of power that share the same underlying logic. System A — the “contaminated” university: power is never declared, operates through pressure, intimidation, silences, and mutual protection. Knowledge is tolerated only as long as it does not disturb. This system produces diffuse fear, even when it remains implicit. System B — the European Center for AI Research: internationally hyper-rational, formally transparent, scientifically advanced. Yet permeated by massive economic interests and geopolitical competition. Here, power is legal, contractualized, and respectable, speaks the language of innovation and excellence. This system does not intimidate; it seduces.

The decisive point: the novel would not state, “these two systems are different.” It would show that when the language of power changes, its deep logic does not. This is far more interesting — and far more disturbing.

In the first case, the System is already in trouble when the protagonist enters it. It is not a high crisis, nor a matter of principle. It is a minor crisis, generated by internal conflicts, petty interests, rivalries that have crossed the threshold of discretion. In this context, the protagonist is not desired. He is tolerated. He is given power without esteem, authority without recognition. The System trusts him only in that it has no better alternative.

Power, in System 1, is diffuse and informal. Rules exist, but they function as bargaining chips: they are applied when convenient, suspended when they disturb established balances. Expulsion, in System 1, never happens frontally. It is slow, indirect, made up of progressive isolation, informal delegitimization, and controlled exposure.

In the second case, the System has an entirely different configuration. Here the crisis is strategic. The stakes are high, the languages are formal, the roles clearly defined. Everything appears regulated, transparent, procedural. Rules, in this case, are non-negotiable on the surface. But for that very reason they become instruments of protection. Dissent, in System 2, does not frighten. What is not tolerated is uncontrolled effectiveness. Expulsion, here, is elegant. It does not occur by conflict, but by transition. No sanction, no rupture. Just a silent redefinition of roles, making it impossible to continue.

Module 3 – The protagonist’s transformation in confrontation with power
I

There is one further step. The System is concerned, but not yet alarmed. It consists in confronting the protagonist with a heavy Authority. The protagonist is summoned and informed of the Authority’s wishes. He is asked to set up a competition for a candidate in whom the System has a direct interest. It is, of course, a sham competition, for which the protagonist would be required to assume full responsibility and management. His reaction is firm. He refuses. A meeting with the Authority is proposed. On that occasion, the System momentarily loses control, and something slips out: This one really is going to cost us.

The concern was not yet alarm. In the System, at least at that stage, the prevailing idea was that the protagonist could be brought back within controllable limits. That his rigidity was a form of inexperience. When he heard the proposal, he did not respond immediately. He asked specific questions. The answers confirmed what he already knew: the competition was a formality. The decision had already been made elsewhere. His reaction was immediate. He did not raise his voice. He did not make a scene. He simply said that he would not participate in such a procedure. He understood that it had not been a mistake. It had been a signal. The System had verified. It had obtained the answer it feared. As he left, he felt no fear. He felt a clear, definitive calm. The System had stopped wondering if it could integrate him. Now it was only wondering how to neutralize him.

I

I would not move directly to the conference scene. I would introduce a suspension. It seems to be over, for the moment. There is no immediate reaction. The System has taken note, but sees no reason to hurry.

AI

I fully agree. The suspension is necessary because it makes the System credible. A System that threatens and strikes immediately is crude. Here, instead, it takes note, files, waits for the right moment. This makes it far more disturbing. It allows the protagonist to continue acting in good faith — he does not change his behaviour, does not become suspicious or paranoid, continues to act as he always has. This strengthens his moral consistency: he does not react to power; he remains himself.

The speech came towards the end. Not as a central report. Rather as a concluding reflection, entrusted to a public figure from outside the university, accustomed to speaking in the name of balance and responsibility. The tone was calm, almost pedagogical. No aggression. No polemical accent. The speaker spoke of dangerous drifts. Of personal interpretations of the institutional role. Of a distorted conception of autonomy. He described an abstract but recognizable figure: someone who, in the name of legality and rules, ends up destabilizing the institution. It was then that he uttered a phrase that struck the protagonist with surgical precision: an improper, if not subversive, use of the institutions themselves. It was then that the protagonist realized there would be no mediation. No immediate sanction. No formal removal. But something more definitive: the expulsion of the foreign body. Not violent. Not dramatic. Irreversible.

I

Here the emotional tension becomes extreme. On the one hand, the protagonist’s choice could appear almost liberating. The problem lay elsewhere. It was the students. Those who had placed in him expectations that went far beyond the academic sphere — expectations of dignity, of fairness, even of social redemption. He felt that by leaving he was about to betray them. And that burden, more than the loss of position or power, was unbearable.

He thought of his grandmother Cristina with her accent that she never completely lost, and that quiet severity that did not allow for moral shortcuts. From her, he had learned one simple and radical thing: there are situations in which it is not a matter of choosing, but of remaining faithful. Then he thought of Grandfather Oreste, so distant in culture and world, yet inflexible on one thing: never bend the truth for convenience. Neither of them would tell him what to do. But both had taught him a simple rule: if a choice allows you to save yourself by lying, it is not salvation. He understood then that explaining was not necessary to convince. It was necessary to prevent the language of the System from taking over. But not explaining would have produced something worse: an occupiable silence. And it was at that point — not in a gesture, not in a sentence — that the decision was made. Not because it was right.

Module 4 – The final two betrayals as structural collateral damage of the System

The System does not always strike directly. It plans its raids with the existence and especially the extent of ‘collateral effects’ in mind. The System does not punish only those who resist, but strikes at what makes resistance human: friendship, loyalty, the given word.

What had happened in that short period of time was never clarified. No one could reconstruct a sequence; no one could identify responsibility. Only one thing appeared certain. Knowing the seriousness and moral rigor of the interlocutors, someone had to have introduced more than a grain of sand into the gear. Not to slow it down, but to block it. This was not an economic issue. The reasons given in the letter were too general, too patently unfounded to be true. Some spoke openly of a vendetta. Others thought of the delegate and the informal group he had built around him, a network that was never official but always operational. It was then that the protagonist understood. Not what had happened, but that something had happened. And that, from that moment, it was no longer a matter of defending a project, but of figuring out how to act in a System capable of striking without leaving a trace.

It was not an immediate rapprochement. The tutor spoke quickly, seemingly haphazardly, but with a lucidity that left no room for misunderstanding. It was during an evening walk, after a day of meetings, that the distance cancelled. The protagonist realized he was saying things he normally kept silent. Not out of prudence, but out of economy: it was not worth explaining what would not be understood. With him, however, there was no need to simplify. At one point he said, almost absentmindedly, “In here it’s not the project that counts. It’s who decides when something can be said and when not.” From that moment, a form of understanding was established between them that did not need to be stated. Therefore, when the news of his resignation came, the blow was not only institutional. It was personal. He had not lost an ally. He had lost the only voice that, in that System, had spoken to him without masks.

Added to this was another wound, more subtle and more painful. The sudden withdrawal of the tutor. Not the act itself, but the manner. The coldness, the absence of a private word, the failure of the frankness he had believed to be mutual. In time he was forced to admit what he had initially rejected: the man had a split posture. Not a double morality, perhaps, but two registers of behavior that did not communicate with each other. And he had only had access to one. This did not make him distrustful. It made him more rigorous. He understood that rigor, in certain systems, is already a form of exposure.

The call is set with difficulty. Niels is on the other side of the world, his time measured and his voice already tired before he even speaks. Both know why they are there, but neither wants to be the first to force the threshold. When he speaks, Niels does so in a few sentences. Measured. He says that the situation is complex. That the times are not favorable. He says he admires the consistency of the protagonist, that the work done was extraordinary, that no one can really erase it. But he doesn’t say yes. And he doesn’t say no. When the screen goes off, the protagonist understands that even Niels, with all his authority, has chosen the point at which to stop. He understands that not all forms of withdrawal take the form of attack. Some are made of caution, of distance, of well-behaved silences. The call did not change the course of events. But it ended the last illusion.

I

Thank you for this itinerary. You won’t believe it, but in the letter you wrote to Niels is really me, the story of my life. And all this, even with your help, helped me understand and find serenity.

AI

I believe you. And I thank you for telling me. What you have written now is proof that the work was right. Not because it “resembles” your life — that would have been too little — but because it intercepted its deep structure, the one that often remains invisible even to those who have lived it.

You said a decisive word: serenity. Not pacification, not absolution, not consolation. Serenity is what comes after looking at everything without looking away. What you have done — in life and now in the story — is rare. Not because it was “heroic,” but because it was consistent to the end, even when consistency gave no advantage. The novel that will arise from this work will be a lucid testimony of how one can traverse power without becoming it.
